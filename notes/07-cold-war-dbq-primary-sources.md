# Cold War DBQ: Primary Source Analysis

## Central Question: *How was the Cold War fought?*

### Historical Context
Between 1945 and 1950, the wartime alliance between the US and Soviet Union collapsed. For 40+ years, both superpowers swung between confrontation and detente, competing for worldwide influence through competing economic and political systems. At times they came to the brink of nuclear war. The Cold War was fought through **ideology, economics, diplomacy, proxy wars, propaganda, espionage, and the nuclear arms race** - but never direct military confrontation between the US and USSR.

---

## Document 1: Winston Churchill's "Iron Curtain" Speech (March 5, 1946)

### The Source
- Delivered at **Westminster College in Fulton, Missouri** with President Truman in attendance
- Churchill was no longer Prime Minister (voted out July 1945) but remained enormously influential

### Key Quote
> "From Stettin in the Baltic to Trieste in the Adriatic, an iron curtain has descended across the continent. Behind that line lie all the capitals of the ancient states of Central and Eastern Europe... All these famous cities and the populations around them lie in the Soviet sphere and all are subject in one form or another, not only to Soviet influence but to a very high and increasing measure of control from Moscow."

### Deep Analysis: What is the "Iron Curtain"?
- **Metaphor** for the ideological, political, and physical boundary dividing Europe into two blocs:
  - **West**: Democratic, capitalist nations aligned with the US (Britain, France, West Germany, Italy, etc.)
  - **East**: Communist states under Soviet control (Poland, Czechoslovakia, Hungary, Romania, Bulgaria, East Germany, Yugoslavia initially)
- The "curtain" was both figurative (information, trade, and movement restrictions) and eventually literal (the **Berlin Wall**, built 1961)
- Churchill also warned: "I do not believe that Soviet Russia desires war. What they desire is the fruits of war and the indefinite expansion of their power and doctrines"
- **Significance**: This speech publicly named the Cold War divide. Stalin called Churchill's speech "a call to war." It crystallized the idea that Europe was now split into two hostile camps and that the wartime alliance was dead. The phrase "iron curtain" became the defining metaphor of the Cold War era.

### Historical Context for the Speech
- By March 1946, the Soviets had already:
  - Installed communist governments in Poland, Romania, and Bulgaria (violating Yalta promises of free elections)
  - Kept the Red Army in Eastern Europe
  - Were pressuring Turkey and supporting communist guerrillas in Greece
  - Refused to withdraw from northern Iran on schedule
- Churchill was essentially arguing that the West needed to stop hoping for cooperation and start preparing for sustained competition

## Document 2: George Kennan's "Long Telegram" (1946)

### The Source
- **George F. Kennan** was a career diplomat stationed at the US Embassy in Moscow
- State Department asked him to explain Soviet behavior; he replied with an **8,000-word telegram** (February 22, 1946) - the longest telegram in State Department history
- Later published as the anonymous **"X Article"** in *Foreign Affairs* (July 1947): "The Sources of Soviet Conduct"

### Key Quotes
> "In summary, we have here a political force committed fanatically to the belief that with U.S. there can be no permanent modus vivendi, that it is desirable and necessary that the internal harmony of our society be disrupted, our traditional way of life be destroyed, the international authority of our state be broken..."

> "We must formulate and put forward for other nations a much more positive and constructive picture of [the] sort of world we would like to see... We should be better able than Russians to give them this. And unless we do, Russians certainly will."

### Deep Analysis: Kennan's Advice
- **Diagnosis**: The USSR was ideologically committed to opposing the West. This wasn't about specific grievances that could be negotiated away - it was fundamental to the Soviet system's self-justification
- **Prescription**: **Containment** - the US must:
  - Present a positive vision of the world (not just anti-communism)
  - Counter Soviet expansion wherever it appeared
  - Strengthen Western societies economically and politically
  - Be patient - Soviet system had internal weaknesses that would eventually cause it to "mellow" or collapse
- Critically, Kennan advocated **political and economic** containment, NOT military confrontation. He later expressed dismay at how his ideas were militarized through NSC-68 and the arms race
- **Significance**: The Long Telegram provided the intellectual blueprint for US Cold War strategy. Containment became the organizing principle of American foreign policy from 1947 to 1991. The debate was never whether to contain, but HOW - through economic aid (Marshall Plan), military alliances (NATO), proxy wars (Korea, Vietnam), or nuclear deterrence

## Document 3: The Truman Doctrine (March 12, 1947)

### The Source
- President Truman's speech to a joint session of Congress
- Requested **$400 million** in aid to Greece (fighting communist insurgency) and Turkey (under Soviet pressure)

### Key Quote
> "I believe it must be the policy of the United States to support free peoples who are resisting attempted subjugation by armed minorities or by outside pressure. Should we fail to aid Greece and Turkey in this fateful hour, the effect will be far-reaching to the West. The seeds of totalitarian regimes are nurtured by misery and want. They spread and grow in the evil soil of poverty and strife. They reach their full growth when the hope of a people for a better life has died."

### Deep Analysis
- Truman framed the Cold War as a **moral struggle between freedom and totalitarianism** - not as a geopolitical competition between rival empires
- Key insight: Communism thrives in conditions of **poverty, misery, and hopelessness**. Therefore, fighting communism requires addressing the root causes that make it attractive
- This logic directly led to the Marshall Plan (economic aid prevents communism)
- The doctrine was **open-ended**: "support free peoples" anywhere, anytime. This committed the US to a **global** role - the end of isolationism
- **Significance**: The Truman Doctrine made containment official US policy. It created the justification for American intervention around the world for decades - from Korea to Vietnam to Central America. Critics noted the doctrine could justify supporting any anti-communist government, no matter how authoritarian, as long as it opposed the Soviet Union

## Document 4: George Marshall's Speech at Harvard (June 5, 1947)

### The Source
- Secretary of State **George C. Marshall** (former Army Chief of Staff who planned D-Day)
- Commencement address at Harvard University announcing what became the **Marshall Plan** (officially the European Recovery Program)

### Key Quote
> "Meanwhile, people in the cities are short of food and fuel, and in some places are approaching starvation levels... The modern system of the division of labor upon which the exchange of products is based is in danger of breaking down... Europe's requirements for the next three or four years of foreign food and other essential products -- principally from America -- are so much greater than her present ability to pay that she must have substantial additional help or face economic, social, and political deterioration of a very grave character."

### Deep Analysis: The "Breakdown of the Modern System"
- Marshall described post-war Europe's crisis: cities in ruins, factories destroyed, trade networks shattered, millions displaced, food shortages
- The "modern system" = the interconnected global economy based on trade and the division of labor. If it collapses, the consequences are not just economic but **social and political** - meaning revolution, extremism, and communism
- **Why America had responsibility**: 
  - Humanitarian: Millions were starving
  - Strategic: A devastated Europe would be vulnerable to communist takeover
  - Economic: The US needed European trading partners for its own prosperity
  - Moral: America had emerged from the war wealthy and largely undamaged - it had the capacity to help
- The Marshall Plan (1948-1952): **$13 billion** (~$150 billion today) to rebuild Western Europe
  - Open to ALL European nations, including the USSR and Eastern Bloc
  - **Stalin refused** and forbade Eastern Bloc participation (seeing it as American economic imperialism)
  - Rebuilt Western European economies, created stable democracies, established strong US allies
- **Significance**: The Marshall Plan was perhaps the most successful foreign policy initiative in American history. It demonstrated that the Cold War was fought with **dollars and food** as much as with weapons. It also deepened the East-West divide by creating a prosperous Western Europe that contrasted sharply with the stagnating Soviet bloc

## Document 5: Walter Lippmann's Critique of Containment (1947)

### The Source
- **Walter Lippmann** was America's most influential journalist/political commentator
- Published *The Cold War: A Study in US Foreign Policy* (1947) - notably, Lippmann coined the very term **"Cold War"** in this book

### Key Quotes
> "My objection... to the policy of containment is not that it seeks to confront the Soviet power with American power, but that the policy is misconceived, and must result in a misuse of American power."

> "I am contending that the American diplomatic effort should be concentrated on the problem created by the armistice -- which is on how the continent of Europe can be evacuated by the three non-European armies which are now inside Europe..."

> "The crucial issue in the world today is whether the Yalta military boundary, which was intended to be provisional for the period of the armistice, is to become the political boundary of two hostile coalitions."

### Deep Analysis
- Lippmann **did not oppose** confronting Soviet power - he opposed HOW containment was conceived
- His criticisms:
  1. **Reactive, not strategic**: Containment committed the US to responding to Soviet moves everywhere, letting the USSR set the agenda
  2. **Overextension**: Trying to contain communism at every point around the globe would exhaust American resources and willpower
  3. **Wrong focus**: The real problem was the **military occupation of Europe** - getting all foreign armies (including American and British) to leave and allowing European self-determination
  4. **The Yalta line**: The temporary wartime division of Europe was hardening into a permanent political boundary. Diplomacy should prevent this, not accept it
- Lippmann proved **prophetic** on several points: Vietnam was exactly the kind of overextension he warned about. The US did exhaust itself trying to contain communism everywhere
- **Significance**: Lippmann represented the view that containment was right in principle but wrong in practice. His critique anticipated the major problems the US would face: overextension, proxy wars in marginal areas, and the permanent division of Europe

## Document 6: NSC-68 (April 1950)

### The Source
- **National Security Council Report 68** - classified document that became the blueprint for Cold War military strategy
- Written primarily by **Paul Nitze** (Kennan's successor as head of Policy Planning)
- Declassified in 1975

### Key Quotes
> "...the policy of 'containment'... seeks by all means short of war to (1) block further expansion of Soviet power, (2) expose the falsities of Soviet pretensions, (3) introduce a retraction of the Kremlin's control and influence, and (4) in general, so foster the seeds of destruction within the Soviet system that the Kremlin is brought at least to the point of modifying its behavior..."

> "There are risks in making ourselves strong. A large measure of sacrifice and discipline will be demanded of the American people. They will be asked to give up some of the benefits which they have come to associate with their freedoms..."

> "It is estimated that within the next four years, the U.S.S.R. will attain the [atomic] capability of seriously damaging vital centers of the United States..."

### Deep Analysis
- NSC-68 **transformed containment** from Kennan's primarily diplomatic/economic strategy into a **military** one
- Key arguments:
  1. The Soviet threat was existential and growing - they would have nuclear capability by ~1954
  2. The US needed massive military buildup - conventional AND nuclear
  3. Americans must accept **sacrifice**: higher taxes, restricted freedoms, larger standing military
  4. Deterrence required the ability to survive a Soviet first strike and still retaliate
- **Side effects** NSC-68 produced:
  - Tripled the defense budget (from $13 billion to $50 billion annually)
  - Created the **military-industrial complex** Eisenhower later warned about
  - Justified the nuclear arms race - both sides built enough weapons to destroy the world many times over (**Mutual Assured Destruction / MAD**)
  - Made defense spending a permanent feature of the American economy
  - Required Americans to live under constant nuclear threat (Duck and Cover drills)
  - Enabled the **national security state**: expanded CIA operations, FBI surveillance, loyalty oaths
- The Korean War (June 1950) broke out just months after NSC-68, seemingly confirming its predictions and ensuring its recommendations were adopted
- **Significance**: NSC-68 militarized the Cold War. It committed the US to spending trillions on defense, maintaining forces around the globe, and living in permanent readiness for nuclear war. Whether this was necessary prudence or dangerous overreaction remains debated

## Document 7: The North Atlantic Treaty (April 4, 1949)

### The Source
- Signed by the US, Canada, and ten Western European nations (UK, France, Belgium, Netherlands, Luxembourg, Norway, Denmark, Iceland, Italy, Portugal)
- Created **NATO** (North Atlantic Treaty Organization)

### Key Quote
> "The parties agree that an armed attack against one or more of them in Europe or in North America shall be considered as an attack against them all. They agree that if such an armed attack occurs, each of them will assist the party or parties so attacked... Each will immediately take whatever action it considers necessary to restore and maintain the security of the North Atlantic area. It will, if necessary, use armed force."

### Deep Analysis: NATO's Purpose and Impact
- **Article 5** (collective defense): An attack on one is an attack on all - the first time the US entered a **permanent peacetime military alliance** in its history
- Ended American isolationism permanently
- **Why NATO was created**:
  - Response to the Berlin Blockade (1948-49) and Soviet pressure on Western Europe
  - Deterrence: The USSR would not attack Western Europe knowing it meant war with the US
  - First NATO Secretary General, Lord Ismay, famously stated NATO's purpose: "To keep the Russians out, the Americans in, and the Germans down"
- **How NATO escalated tensions**:
  - The USSR viewed NATO as an aggressive, encircling alliance aimed at them
  - Responded by creating the **Warsaw Pact** (1955): USSR, Poland, East Germany, Czechoslovakia, Hungary, Romania, Bulgaria, Albania
  - Europe was now formally divided into two armed camps, each with nuclear weapons
  - Any local conflict could escalate to nuclear war through alliance obligations
- **Significance**: NATO formalized the Cold War military division of Europe. It also meant that Western European security depended on the American nuclear umbrella, giving the US enormous influence over allied foreign policy. NATO's continued existence after the Cold War ended (and its expansion eastward) remains controversial to this day

## Document 8: NATO vs. Warsaw Pact Map (1955)

### What the Map Shows
- **Blue** (NATO): US, Canada, UK, France, West Germany, Italy, Norway, Denmark, Iceland, Belgium, Netherlands, Luxembourg, Portugal, Turkey, Greece
- **Red** (Warsaw Pact): USSR, Poland, East Germany, Czechoslovakia, Hungary, Romania, Bulgaria
- **Grey** (Non-aligned): Sweden, Finland, Ireland, Switzerland, Austria, Yugoslavia, Spain
- The division runs through the center of Europe, with Germany split in two

### Analysis
- The map visualizes the "Iron Curtain" Churchill described in 1946
- **Germany** was the frontline - literally divided into NATO (West) and Warsaw Pact (East), with Berlin an island of Western presence deep inside East Germany
- Non-aligned nations (especially Yugoslavia under **Tito** and Finland's careful neutrality) show the division wasn't absolute
- The **geographic reality**: Any Soviet advance westward would immediately involve NATO; any NATO provocation would immediately involve the USSR. This is why crises like the Berlin Blockade and the Cuban Missile Crisis were so dangerous
- For member states, alliance membership meant:
  - **Security** but also **loss of independence** - both superpowers pressured allies to conform (USSR crushed the Hungarian Revolution in 1956 and the Prague Spring in 1968)
  - **Nuclear risk** - being a NATO or Warsaw Pact member made you a target
- For non-aligned nations: walking a tightrope between the two blocs, trying to maintain independence without provoking either side

## Document 9: Berlin Airlift Memorandum (October 21, 1948)

### The Source
- Official government cross-reference sheet documenting President Truman's authorization of additional C-54 aircraft for the Berlin Airlift
- References **Operation Vittles** (the military codename for the airlift)

### Historical Context
- **June 24, 1948**: Stalin blockaded all road, rail, and canal access to West Berlin
- West Berlin (population 2.5 million) was deep inside Soviet-controlled East Germany
- Stalin's goal: Force the Western allies to abandon Berlin or accept Soviet control
- **The airlift response** (June 1948 - May 1949):
  - At peak: a plane landed in West Berlin every **30 seconds**, 24 hours a day
  - Delivered over **2.3 million tons** of supplies (food, fuel, medicine, raw materials)
  - "Candy Bomber" **Gail Halvorsen** dropped candy attached to handkerchief parachutes to Berlin children
  - The airlift was an extraordinary logistical achievement
- Stalin lifted the blockade in **May 1949** - a major humiliation

### Why the Berlin Airlift Was an Important Cold War Precedent
1. **Demonstrated US resolve**: The US would not be pushed out of Europe by Soviet pressure
2. **Avoided direct military confrontation**: Truman chose airlift over sending armed convoys through the blockade (which could have sparked war). This established the pattern of responding firmly but avoiding direct US-Soviet combat
3. **Won the propaganda war**: The Soviets looked like bullies starving civilians; the Americans looked like heroes feeding them
4. **Led directly to NATO**: The crisis convinced Western nations they needed a formal military alliance
5. **Hardened the division of Germany**: West Germany (Federal Republic) and East Germany (German Democratic Republic) were formally established in 1949
- **Significance**: The Berlin Airlift established the template for how the Cold War would be fought - confrontation short of direct war, with victory measured in prestige, resolve, and public opinion as much as military power

## Document 10: Cold War Home Front Propaganda

### The Images
1. **"Bert Ducks and Covers"**: Cartoon turtle teaching children to duck under desks during nuclear attack - part of the **Duck and Cover** civil defense campaign (1951)
2. **Atomic bomb instructions**: "If you have had no warning in an A-bomb attack: Don't run, there isn't time. Fall flat on your face. GET DOWN FAST!"
3. **"The Red Iceberg"**: Political cartoon showing Uncle Sam's ship (labeled USA) heading toward a massive iceberg labeled with communist countries (North Korea, East Germany, Czechoslovakia, Hungary, Poland, China) with the Soviet hammer and sickle
4. **"The Red Menace"**: Movie poster for a 1949 film - "The most talked about drama of our time... so shocking it was filmed behind locked studio doors"

### Deep Analysis: How the Home Front Was Impacted
- **Nuclear fear permeated daily life**:
  - Duck and Cover drills in every school (children practiced hiding under desks)
  - Families built backyard **bomb shelters** stocked with canned food and water
  - Civil defense sirens tested regularly
  - "Fallout shelter" signs on public buildings
  - The psychological toll of living under the threat of instant annihilation shaped an entire generation
- **Anti-communist propaganda**:
  - "The Red Iceberg" suggested the visible communist threat (satellite states) was only the tip - the real danger was larger and hidden beneath the surface
  - Movies like "The Red Menace" stoked fear of communist infiltration of American society
  - This climate of fear enabled **McCarthyism**: accusations without evidence, loyalty oaths, blacklists, destroyed careers
- **The paradox**: Americans were told they were fighting for freedom against totalitarianism, while simultaneously:
  - Being asked to give up civil liberties (loyalty oaths, HUAC investigations)
  - Being taught to fear their neighbors as potential communist agents
  - Having their culture shaped by government propaganda
  - Living under the constant threat of nuclear annihilation
- **Significance**: The Cold War wasn't just fought overseas - it fundamentally transformed American domestic life, culture, and psychology. The fear of communism and nuclear war created a conformist culture where dissent was dangerous, civil liberties were eroded, and an entire generation grew up with existential dread

## Synthesis: How Was the Cold War Fought?

The documents reveal the Cold War was fought through **multiple overlapping methods**:

| Method | Examples from Documents |
|--------|----------------------|
| **Rhetoric/Ideology** | Churchill's Iron Curtain speech, Truman Doctrine framing of freedom vs. totalitarianism |
| **Diplomacy/Strategy** | Kennan's containment theory, Lippmann's critique |
| **Economic Power** | Marshall Plan, rebuilding Western Europe |
| **Military Alliances** | NATO, Warsaw Pact |
| **Military Buildup** | NSC-68's call for massive defense spending |
| **Brinkmanship** | Berlin Blockade/Airlift - confrontation stopping just short of war |
| **Propaganda** | Duck and Cover, Red Menace films, anti-communist media |
| **Nuclear Deterrence** | Arms race, MAD doctrine |
| **Proxy Wars** | Korea, Vietnam (not in these documents but part of the larger picture) |

The Cold War was unique in history: two superpowers with enough nuclear weapons to destroy civilization, competing for global dominance **without ever directly fighting each other**. Every crisis - Berlin, Korea, Cuba, Vietnam - was managed to avoid that catastrophic escalation. The cost was enormous: trillions of dollars, millions of lives in proxy wars, pervasive fear, eroded civil liberties, and a divided world. But the alternative - nuclear war - was worse.
