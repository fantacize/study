# The Cold War: Spring Review

## Post-War Planning (1944-1945)

### Bretton Woods Conference (July 1944)
- Established the **International Monetary Fund (IMF)** and **World Bank**
- Globalized free trade under American leadership
- Created a new international economic order with the US dollar as the reserve currency
- The Soviet Union participated but never ratified the agreements, seeing them as tools of Western capitalism
- **Significance**: Set the stage for economic tensions between capitalist West and communist East

### Yalta Conference (February 1945)
- **Big Three**: FDR, Stalin, Churchill met in Crimea
- Agreements reached:
  - Plan victory strategy for final defeat of Germany
  - Divide Germany into occupation zones (US, UK, France, USSR)
  - Stalin promised free and fair elections in Eastern Europe
  - USSR would join the war against Japan
- **Seeds of tension**: Stalin had no intention of allowing truly free elections in Eastern Europe; the vague language gave him room to install communist puppet governments

### Potsdam Conference (July-August 1945)
- **Big Three changed**: Truman replaced FDR (who died April 1945), Attlee replaced Churchill mid-conference
- Finalized terms of Germany's surrender and occupation
- Key tensions:
  - Truman far more confrontational toward Stalin than FDR had been
  - Truman hinted America had a "new weapon" (atomic bomb, tested July 16, 1945)
  - Truman criticized Stalin's treatment of Poland
  - Disagreements over German reparations
- **Significance**: Marked the beginning of mutual suspicion that would define the Cold War

## America's Foreign Policy Framework

### George Kennan's "Long Telegram" (1946)
- Sent from US Embassy in Moscow to Washington
- Key argument: The Soviet Union was a "political force committed fanatically to the belief that with the U.S. there can be no permanent modus vivendi"
- Recommended **containment** - the US must formulate a positive vision of the world and actively counter Soviet expansion
- Did NOT advocate military confrontation but rather political, economic, and ideological competition
- **Impact**: Became the intellectual foundation of US Cold War policy for 40+ years

### Truman Doctrine (March 12, 1947)
- Truman's speech to Congress requesting $400 million in aid to Greece and Turkey
- Key quote: "I believe it must be the policy of the United States to support free peoples who are resisting attempted subjugation by armed minorities or by outside pressure"
- Framed the Cold War as a struggle between freedom and totalitarianism
- **Significance**: Committed the US to actively intervening worldwide to prevent communist expansion; marked the end of American isolationism

### Marshall Plan (June 1947)
- Secretary of State George Marshall's speech at Harvard
- Europe was devastated: food shortages, fuel shortages, cities in ruins
- Proposed massive American economic aid to rebuild Europe
- Approximately **$13 billion** (about $150 billion in today's dollars) distributed 1948-1952
- Open to ALL European nations, including the USSR (Stalin refused and forbade Eastern Bloc participation)
- **Dual purpose**: Humanitarian aid AND strategic tool to prevent communism from taking root in desperate, impoverished nations
- **Significance**: Rebuilt Western Europe, created strong US allies and trading partners, deepened the East-West divide

### NSC-68 (April 1950)
- National Security Council Report 68 - classified document that became the blueprint for Cold War military policy
- Key points:
  - Containment must use "all means short of war" to block Soviet expansion
  - Predicted the USSR would have atomic capability by 1954
  - Called for massive military buildup - conventional and nuclear
  - Demanded Americans sacrifice some freedoms and accept higher taxes for security
- **Significance**: Transformed containment from a primarily diplomatic/economic strategy into a military one; justified the massive peacetime military buildup (military-industrial complex)

## Early Cold War Crises

### Berlin Blockade & Airlift (1948-1949)
- Stalin blockaded all road, rail, and canal access to West Berlin (which was deep inside Soviet-controlled East Germany)
- Goal: Force Western allies to abandon Berlin or accept Soviet currency/control
- US/UK response: Massive airlift - flew supplies into West Berlin for **11 months**
  - At peak, planes landed every 30 seconds
  - Delivered over 2.3 million tons of supplies
- Stalin lifted the blockade May 1949, a major humiliation
- **Significance**: First major direct confrontation of the Cold War; demonstrated US resolve; led directly to the creation of NATO (April 1949) and the formal division of Germany into West (FRG) and East (GDR)

### Two Major Shifts of 1949
1. **China becomes communist** (October 1949)
   - Mao Zedong's Communist forces defeated Chiang Kai-shek's Nationalists after decades of civil war
   - Created the People's Republic of China
   - "Who lost China?" became a major domestic political question in the US
   - Dramatically expanded the perceived communist threat
2. **USSR tests an atomic bomb** (August 1949)
   - Ended America's nuclear monopoly years earlier than expected
   - Sparked the nuclear arms race
   - Led Truman to approve development of the hydrogen bomb (1,000x more powerful)
   - Combined with China, created near-panic about communist expansion

## The Cold War on the Home Front

### The Red Scare / Second Red Scare (late 1940s-1950s)
- Fear of communist infiltration of American society
- Key events:
  - **Hollywood Ten (1947)**: Ten screenwriters/directors refused to testify before HUAC (House Un-American Activities Committee) about communist ties; jailed for contempt; blacklisted from the industry
  - **McCarthyism / McCarthy Hearings (1950-1954)**: Senator Joseph McCarthy claimed to have lists of communists in the State Department; conducted aggressive, often baseless investigations; ruined careers through accusation alone; finally discredited during the Army-McCarthy hearings (1954) when attorney Joseph Welch asked, "Have you no sense of decency?"
  - **McCarran-Walter Act (1952)**: Immigration law that allowed deportation of immigrants suspected of communist sympathies; maintained racial quotas
- **Duck and Cover**: Civil defense campaigns taught children to hide under desks during nuclear attacks; bomb shelters became common
- **Significance**: Demonstrated how foreign policy fears could erode domestic civil liberties; created a culture of conformity and suspicion

### The Korean War (1950-1953)
- Korea divided at 38th parallel after Japan's defeat in 1945: Soviet zone (North) and American zone (South)
- June 1950: North Korean army invaded the South to unite Korea under communism
- US intervened under UN authority (USSR was boycotting the Security Council)
- General MacArthur pushed North Koreans back nearly to the Chinese border
- China entered the war with massive forces, pushing UN troops back south
- MacArthur wanted to use nuclear weapons against China; Truman fired him
- War ended in stalemate; armistice signed July 1953 at roughly the same 38th parallel border
- **Casualties**: ~37,000 American dead; 2-3 million Korean and Chinese dead
- **Significance**: First "hot" war of the Cold War; established the precedent of limited war (fighting without using nuclear weapons); Korea remains divided today
