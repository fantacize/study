# The End of the 1960s and The Conservative Turn: Nixon

## The Rights Revolution of the 1960s (Review)

### The Women's Rights Movement
- **Betty Friedan** published *The Feminine Mystique* (1963)
  - Chapter One: "The Problem That Has No Name"
  - Described the dissatisfaction of suburban housewives who felt trapped in domestic roles
  - "Each suburban wife struggled with it alone... she was afraid to ask even of herself the silent question -- 'Is this all?'"
- Letters to Friedan showed widespread frustration among educated women confined to homemaking
- **National Organization for Women (NOW)** founded 1966 to push for workplace equality, reproductive rights, and equal legal status
- Second-wave feminism went beyond voting rights to challenge gender roles in every aspect of society

### The Sexual Revolution
- Counterculture movement combined with second-wave feminism reshaped attitudes around personal freedom and choice
- Birth control pill (approved 1960) gave women new control over reproduction
- Challenged traditional morality around marriage and relationships

### Gay Rights Movement
- Gay and lesbian people faced persecution: fired from jobs, arrested, institutionalized
- **Stonewall Inn Raid (1969)** - Police raided a gay bar in Greenwich Village, New York
  - 5 days of protests and resistance followed
  - Led to many people "coming out" publicly
  - Made gay/lesbian pride more visible and militant
  - Began the tradition of annual gay pride marches
- **Significance**: Stonewall marks the beginning of the modern LGBTQ+ rights movement

### Latino and American Indian Rights
- **Chicano Movement**: Modeled after the Black Power movement
  - **Cesar Chavez** organized California farmworkers into the United Farm Workers union
  - Used boycotts and strikes to win labor contracts
- **American Indian Movement (AIM)**: Founded 1968
  - Used collective action modeled on the civil rights movement
  - **Occupation of Alcatraz (1969)**: Native activists occupied the former prison island for 19 months to draw attention to broken treaties and federal neglect

### Environmentalism
- **Rachel Carson** published *Silent Spring* (1962)
  - Warned about the dangers of pesticides (especially DDT) and chemical contamination
  - Described how chemicals entered the food chain and accumulated in living tissue
  - Sparked the modern environmental movement
- Factors behind the new environmental focus: nuclear testing fallout, industrial pollution, urban smog, chemical contamination of water and soil
- Led to the creation of the EPA (1970) and major environmental legislation

## The Conservative Backlash

### Why Conservatism Grew
- Many Americans were alarmed by:
  - Urban unrest and rising crime rates
  - Antiwar protests and counterculture
  - Expansion of federal government power
  - Perceived moral decline
  - Supreme Court decisions on prayer, rights of accused
- The counterculture and rights revolution provoked a strong reaction

### Young Americans for Freedom (YAF) and the Sharon Statement (1960)
- Conservative student organization founded in response to counterculture radicalism
- **Sharon Statement** (September 11, 1960) outlined their beliefs:
  - Individual free will and God-given rights are paramount
  - Political freedom requires economic freedom (free markets)
  - Government should be limited to national defense, internal order, and justice
  - The Constitution's genius is the division of powers
  - Market economy is the best system for human needs
  - International Communism is the greatest threat to liberty
  - The US should seek victory over, not coexistence with, Communism
- **Significance**: Showed that the 1960s were not only about liberal/radical movements; a powerful conservative counter-movement was forming simultaneously

## Richard Nixon's Rise

### The 1968 Election Context
- America was in crisis: Vietnam, assassinations (MLK and RFK in 1968), urban riots, campus protests
- LBJ chose not to run for re-election
- Nixon ran as the candidate of order and stability

### Nixon's Acceptance Speech (Republican Convention, August 8, 1968)
- "When the strongest nation in the world can be tied down for four years in a war in Vietnam with no end in sight; when the richest nation in the world can't manage its own economy; when the nation with the greatest tradition of the rule of law is plagued by unprecedented lawlessness... then it's time for new leadership"
- **Strategy**: Linked Vietnam failure, economic problems, and social disorder to Democratic leadership

### The "Silent Majority" (November 3, 1969)
- Nixon's nationally televised address on Vietnam
- Appealed to "the great silent majority of my fellow Americans" for support
- Argued that withdrawing from Vietnam would embolden totalitarianism
- Framed antiwar protesters as a loud minority vs. the patriotic majority
- **Significance**: Coined a powerful political concept - the idea that most Americans were conservative but quiet, while radicals got all the attention

### The Southern Strategy
- **Kevin Phillips** (Nixon strategist) explained the approach in *The New York Times* (1970):
  - Republicans would never get more than 10-20% of the Black vote "and they don't need any more than that"
  - As more Black voters registered as Democrats in the South, white Southerners would leave the Democratic Party and become Republicans
  - "That's where the votes are"
- **Significance**: Deliberately exploited racial resentment to realign Southern white voters from Democrat to Republican; this realignment reshaped American politics permanently

## Nixon's Presidency

### Vietnam Policy: Vietnamization
- Gradually transferred combat responsibility to South Vietnamese forces
- Ended the military draft (to reduce domestic opposition)
- Simultaneously expanded the air war: secret bombing of Cambodia and Laos
- Negotiated Paris Peace Accords (January 1973)

### Nixon's Letter Exchange with Ho Chi Minh (1969)
- **Nixon to Ho Chi Minh** (July 15, 1969): Expressed desire for "a just peace," acknowledged the war had gone on too long
- **Ho Chi Minh to Nixon** (August 25, 1969): Called the war "aggression" against Vietnamese people; demanded US withdraw troops and respect Vietnamese self-determination
- **Significance**: Shows the fundamental disagreement - Nixon framed it as preventing communist expansion; Ho Chi Minh framed it as a war of national liberation against foreign occupation

### Detente and Foreign Policy Achievements
- Opened relations with **China** (1972 visit to Beijing) - first US president to visit communist China
- Signed arms control agreements with the **Soviet Union** (SALT I)
- Second Inaugural Address (January 20, 1973): Claimed 1972 as "the year of the greatest progress since the end of World War II toward a lasting peace in the world"
- **Significance**: Nixon's foreign policy was genuinely innovative - triangulating between China and USSR was a major strategic achievement

### Economic Challenges
- **Stagflation**: Rising inflation combined with economic stagnation
- Consumer Price Index (1968-1975) showed sharp increases in food, fuel, and transportation costs
- 1973 Oil Crisis (OPEC embargo) made everything worse
- Nixon imposed wage and price controls (unusual for a Republican)

### The Watergate Scandal
- June 1972: Burglars connected to Nixon's re-election campaign broke into Democratic National Committee headquarters at the Watergate complex
- Nixon administration attempted to cover up its involvement
- Investigation revealed:
  - Secret White House taping system
  - Enemies lists
  - Use of FBI and IRS against political opponents
  - Obstruction of justice
- **Saturday Night Massacre** (October 1973): Nixon fired the special prosecutor investigating him
- Herblock cartoon (October 24, 1973): Depicted Nixon trying to cover up scandals involving aides Haldeman, Mitchell, Ehrlichman
- Supreme Court ordered Nixon to release the tapes (*United States v. Nixon*, 1974)
- The tapes proved Nixon had directed the cover-up
- **August 9, 1974**: Nixon resigned rather than face certain impeachment
- **Significance**: Deepened American cynicism about government; led to reforms in campaign finance and government ethics; the suffix "-gate" became shorthand for political scandal

## Key Question for Study
**To what degree did the actions and policies of the Nixon Administration represent a change from other mid-twentieth century presidents?**

Consider:
- Foreign policy: Detente vs. Cold War confrontation
- Domestic policy: Law and order vs. Great Society liberalism
- Vietnam: Escalation (LBJ) vs. Vietnamization (Nixon)
- Political strategy: Southern Strategy realignment
- Use of executive power: Watergate as abuse
