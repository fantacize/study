# The Reagan Era: The Conservative Resurgence

## The New Right: Origins of the Conservative Movement

### Why Americans Turned Right (Late 1970s)
By the late 1970s, middle and working-class Americans — especially in the South — had grown tired of liberal programs and trends:

- **Busing** — *Swann v. Charlotte-Mecklenburg Board of Education* (1971): Supreme Court allowed forced busing to desegregate schools. Massively unpopular among white parents in both the South and North (Boston busing crisis 1974-76 was especially violent)
- **Affirmative Action** — *Regents of Univ. of California v. Bakke* (1978): Court upheld race-conscious admissions while striking down quotas. Many whites felt discriminated against
- **No Established Prayer in Schools** — *Engel v. Vitale* (1962): Banned mandatory school prayer. Deeply unpopular among religious Americans for decades
- **Unfunded Federal Mandates**: Federal regulations and laws that states/localities had to implement but Congress didn't pay for
- **"Degeneration" of Youth**: Counterculture, drugs, rock music, and — most significantly — *Roe v. Wade* (1973) legalizing abortion

### The Conservative Coalition
The American conservative movement fused two distinct wings:
- **Religious conservatives**: Focus on abortion, school prayer, family values, "traditional" morality
- **Fiscal (economic) conservatives**: Focus on low taxes, deregulation, balanced budgets, free markets

These wings did not always agree — evangelicals were often economically populist, and libertarian fiscal conservatives often had progressive social views — but together they formed a powerful political coalition.

### The Southern Realignment
- The 1970s saw the South shift decisively **from Democratic to Republican**
- Historical background: The "**Solid South**" had voted Democratic since Reconstruction (1877)
- Civil Rights Act (1964) + Voting Rights Act (1965) + Nixon's Southern Strategy broke the Democratic hold
- By 1980, Reagan swept the South — a region Carter (himself a Georgia Democrat) had won in 1976

## The Moral Majority

### Founded by Jerry Falwell (1979)
- **Jerry Falwell** (1933-2007): Televangelist and pastor of Thomas Road Baptist Church (Lynchburg, VA); founded **Liberty University**
- Moral Majority was a **religious organization explicitly aimed at political change**
- Claimed to represent ordinary Americans who supported "Traditional" and "Mainstream American" values

### Goals
- Reverse *Roe v. Wade* (abortion)
- Restore prayer in public schools
- Oppose the Equal Rights Amendment (ERA)
- Oppose gay rights
- Strengthen family values
- Support Israel (end-times theology drove Christian Zionism)
- Strong national defense against "godless communism"

### Tactics
- **Voter registration drives** in evangelical churches
- **Candidate scorecards** rating politicians on moral issues
- **Fundraising** via direct mail and televangelism
- Mobilized 4 million members at its peak
- Pioneered the use of **church networks as political organizing infrastructure**

### Significance
- Moral Majority dissolved in 1989 but its impact was permanent
- Transformed white evangelical Christians into **the most reliable Republican voting bloc**
- Made social issues (abortion, prayer, gay rights) central to national politics
- Blueprint for later Christian right organizations: Christian Coalition (Pat Robertson, 1989), Focus on the Family (James Dobson), Family Research Council

## Ronald Reagan (President 1981-1989)

### Background
- Born 1911 in Tampico, Illinois
- **B-movie actor** in Hollywood (1937-1965) — made ~50 films, none great
- President of the **Screen Actors Guild** during McCarthy era; cooperated with HUAC, named suspected communists
- **General Electric Theater** host (1954-1962) — traveled the country giving speeches for GE, refining conservative message
- Switched from Democrat to Republican in 1962
- **"A Time for Choosing"** speech (October 27, 1964) — televised speech supporting Barry Goldwater; made Reagan a national conservative figure
- **Governor of California** (1967-1975): Tough on Berkeley protesters, cut taxes, signed liberal abortion law (later disavowed)
- **Oldest president** to take office (69 years old) — held that record until Trump (70) in 2017

### Core Beliefs
- **Lower taxes**
- **Traditional American values**
- **Military preparedness** — especially against the USSR
- Called the Soviet Union **"The Evil Empire"** (March 8, 1983 speech to National Association of Evangelicals)

### 1980 Election Context
- Conservative resurgence + bad late-1970s economy + Iran hostage crisis + Carter's perceived weakness = Reagan landslide
- Defeated Carter **489-49 electoral votes**, 50.7% to 41.0% popular vote
- Third-party candidate **John Anderson** got 6.6%
- Reagan won 44 states

### First Inaugural Address (January 20, 1981)
Most famous quote:
> "Government is not the solution to our problem; government is the problem. From time to time we've been tempted to believe that society has become too complex to be managed by self-rule, that government by an elite group is superior to government for, by, and of the people. Well, if no one among us is capable of governing himself, then who among us has the capacity to govern someone else?"

**Significance**: This was the **philosophical foundation of Reaganism**. It rejected the New Deal/Great Society premise that activist government was the solution to problems. It framed government itself — the federal government especially — as inherently illegitimate, inefficient, and an obstacle to freedom. Everything Reagan did domestically flowed from this premise.

## "Reaganomics": Supply-Side Economics

### The Theory
Also called **"trickle-down economics"**:
1. **Cut taxes** (especially on the wealthy and corporations)
2. Cut **domestic and social spending** to pay for tax cuts
3. Reduce **government regulation** of industry
4. Result: Private sector invests more → increased production, jobs, prosperity → wealth "trickles down"

### Why Cut Taxes on the Rich Specifically?
- Theory: The wealthy have more money to invest. If they keep more of it (rather than paying taxes), they'll invest in businesses, creating jobs and growth
- **Laffer Curve**: Economist **Arthur Laffer** argued that at very high tax rates, cutting rates could actually *increase* revenue by stimulating growth
- Critics called this "**voodoo economics**" — term coined by **George H.W. Bush** during 1980 Republican primary (before he became Reagan's VP)

### The Economic Recovery Tax Act (1981)
- Top marginal tax rate cut: **70% (1980) → 50% (1981) → 28% (1986)**
- This was a **reversal of New Deal-era tax structure**
- Corporate tax rates cut
- Capital gains taxes reduced
- Indexed tax brackets to inflation (prevented "bracket creep")

### Deregulation
- Reduced government regulation of industry
- Cut staff and enforcement at EPA, OSHA, Interior Department
- Ended **Fairness Doctrine** (1987) — FCC rule requiring broadcasters to present controversial issues fairly. Opened door to partisan talk radio (Rush Limbaugh) and eventually Fox News
- Deregulated banking (savings & loan industry) — contributed to **S&L Crisis** ($160 billion taxpayer bailout, 1989)

### Military Spending Offset
- While domestic spending was cut, **military spending DRAMATICALLY increased** (see below)
- Defense budget went from ~$100B to ~$300B/year
- Net effect: government kept spending, just shifted from domestic to military

## Results of Reaganomics

### The Economic Trajectory
- **1981-1982**: Economy sputtered badly — **severe recession** (unemployment hit 10.8%, highest since Great Depression)
- **1983 onward**: Strong recovery, sustained growth through the decade
- By 1984: Clear improvement, enabling Reagan's "Morning Again in America" campaign
- **1984 re-election**: Reagan won **49 states** (525 electoral votes) vs. Walter Mondale's 13 (only Minnesota + DC)

### Who Benefited
- **Rich got richer**: The "**yuppies**" (young urban professionals) — symbol of the 1980s
- Wall Street boomed; financial sector ballooned
- Share of national income going to top 10% climbed sharply after 1980 (reversing 40 years of stability)

### Who Lost
- **Number of poor Americans increased**
- **Middle-class standard of living stagnated** or slightly decreased
- Manufacturing jobs disappeared as factories closed or moved overseas (Rust Belt decline)
- **Homelessness exploded**: Visible homeless populations in American cities for first time since Depression
  - Activist **Mitch Snyder** (Community for Creative Non-Violence) led hunger strikes to protest homelessness
  - Deinstitutionalization of mental patients (started under Kennedy, continued under Reagan) combined with rising housing costs and cut social programs

### National Debt
- **Budget deficits exploded** from 1981-1986
- National debt went from **$908 billion (1980) to $2.7 trillion (1989)** — **tripled**
- Reagan — despite campaigning on balanced budgets — added more debt than all previous presidents combined
- This was the **paradox of Reaganomics**: tax cuts + military buildup ≠ balanced budget. Something had to give, and it was fiscal discipline

### Inequality Chart (1917-1997)
- Share of national income going to richest 10%:
  - **1920s**: ~45% (Gilded Age peak)
  - **1940s-1970s**: ~32% (postwar egalitarian era)
  - **After 1980**: Climbed steadily — back to ~44% by late 1990s
- Reagan's tax and regulatory changes **reversed four decades of reduced inequality**

## The War on Drugs

### Nixon's Declaration (1971)
- **Nixon declared "War on Drugs"** — identified drug abuse as "public enemy number one"
- Created DEA (1973)
- **Ehrlichman confession** (John Ehrlichman, Nixon aide, admitted in 1994 interview): "We knew we couldn't make it illegal to be either against the war or black, but by getting the public to associate the hippies with marijuana and blacks with heroin, and then criminalizing both heavily, we could disrupt those communities... Did we know we were lying about the drugs? Of course we did"

### Reagan's Escalation
- **Reagan dramatically expanded** the War on Drugs
- **Nancy Reagan's "Just Say No"** campaign (1982+)
- **Sentencing Reform Act (1984)**: Created mandatory minimum sentences, federal sentencing guidelines
- **Anti-Drug Abuse Act (1986)**: Created infamous **100:1 crack-to-powder cocaine sentencing disparity** — 5 grams of crack = 500 grams of powder in sentencing terms
  - Crack was cheaper, used in poor/Black communities; powder cocaine was used by wealthier/whiter populations
  - Result: Massive racial disparities in drug sentencing
- **Anti-Drug Abuse Act (1988)**: Created Office of National Drug Control Policy (drug czar)

### Mass Incarceration
- **Incarcerated Americans**:
  - 1970: ~300,000
  - 1985: ~750,000
  - 2000: ~2 million
  - 2008: ~2.4 million
- US became the **world's largest prison population**
- **Racial disparities**:
  - **Blacks nearly 4x more likely than whites** to be arrested for marijuana possession (despite similar use rates)
  - Black youth drug possession arrests peaked ~400/100,000; white youth ~200/100,000
  - Black youth drug sales arrests peaked ~280/100,000; white youth ~40/100,000
- **Black male incarceration rate** increased roughly **5x** from 1970 to peak in 2007-2010

### The Crack Epidemic
- Crack cocaine devastated Black communities in 1980s
- **Murder rate for Black males ages 15-24** spiked from ~85/100,000 (1980) to ~170/100,000 (early 1990s)
- Killer Mike ("Reagan" song, 2012) and Jay-Z among artists who later critiqued Reagan-era drug policy as targeting Black communities

### The Contras-Crack Connection (Gary Webb, "Dark Alliance")
- **Gary Webb**, investigative journalist for *San Jose Mercury News*
- **1996 series**: "Dark Alliance: The Story Behind the Crack Explosion"
- Alleged CIA-backed **Contras** in Nicaragua funded their war partly through **cocaine trafficking into US inner cities**, fueling the 1980s crack epidemic
- Specifically tied Bay Area drug dealer to Crips and Bloods in LA, who bought from Contra-linked Nicaraguans
- Webb was **attacked** by major newspapers (*NYT*, *LA Times*, *Washington Post*) but later **CIA Inspector General's report (1998)** confirmed CIA had worked with known drug traffickers
- Webb's career was destroyed; died 2004 (officially suicide)
- **Significance**: Even if details were contested, the broader pattern — US government tolerated/enabled drug trafficking by anti-communist allies while waging domestic War on Drugs — was documented

## The Cold War Endgame: Military Buildup

### Reagan's Military Strategy
- **Increased military spending from $100 billion → $300 billion per year** during his presidency
- Both **nuclear and conventional** arms expanded
- Believed (correctly) that the USSR could not match this spending without collapsing its economy
- Reagan's theory: confront the USSR directly, bankrupt them, and they will collapse

### Key Programs
- **B-1 bomber** revived (Carter had cancelled)
- **MX Peacekeeper** ICBMs deployed
- **Pershing II** missiles deployed in Europe (1983) — could hit Moscow in 6 minutes
- **600-ship Navy** target
- Massive expansion of special forces and intelligence operations

### The Strategic Defense Initiative (SDI) / "Star Wars" (March 23, 1983)
- Proposed space-based **anti-missile defense system**
- Would use lasers, satellites, and missile interceptors to shoot down incoming Soviet ICBMs
- Nickname **"Star Wars"** came from senators and media (after the 1977 film)
- **Technical skeptics**: Many scientists said the technology was impossible with 1980s technology
- **Strategic impact**: Whether SDI could work didn't matter — the USSR had to try to match it, which it could not afford
- May have been **"the last straw"** economically for the Soviet Union

## Reagan's Anti-Communist Interventions

### Nicaragua: The Contras
- **Sandinistas** (left-wing revolutionaries) overthrew US-backed **Somoza** dictatorship in 1979
- Reagan administration funded the **Contras** — anti-Sandinista rebels — to try to overthrow the Sandinista government
- Contras were accused of massive human rights abuses (torture, murder, rape of civilians)
- **Boland Amendment** (1982, strengthened 1984): Congress prohibited US funding of the Contras
- Administration found illegal workarounds → Iran-Contra Affair (see below)

### Grenada (October 1983)
- US **invaded** tiny Caribbean island of Grenada (Operation Urgent Fury)
- Overthrew **leftist Marxist government**
- Justification: Protect American medical students; stop Cuban influence
- First "victory" since Vietnam — boosted American military confidence
- Condemned by UN General Assembly (108-9 vote) but celebrated at home

### El Salvador
- Supported **right-wing government** fighting leftist (FMLN) rebels
- US-backed Salvadoran military committed atrocities:
  - **Archbishop Oscar Romero** assassinated by death squads (March 24, 1980)
  - **El Mozote massacre** (December 1981): Salvadoran army (trained by US) massacred ~800+ civilians
- War killed ~75,000 Salvadorans; drove massive migration to US

### Afghanistan
- **Soviet Union invaded Afghanistan** (December 1979)
- US funded **Mujahideen** (Afghan resistance fighters) — **Operation Cyclone**
- Massive CIA program — ~$3 billion over the war
- Saudi Arabia matched US funding dollar-for-dollar
- Weapons funneled through Pakistan's ISI intelligence
- Stinger missiles (provided 1986) let Mujahideen shoot down Soviet helicopters — turned the tide
- Soviet Union withdrew in 1989 after 10 years, ~15,000 Soviet dead
- **Blowback**: Many Mujahideen fighters later joined Al-Qaeda and the Taliban; **Osama bin Laden** was among the foreign fighters involved

## The Iran-Contra Affair

### What Happened
- Senior Reagan administration officials **secretly sold arms to Iran** (which was under a US arms embargo)
- Goals:
  1. **Secure release of American hostages** held by Iran-aligned Hezbollah in Lebanon
  2. **Fund the Contras** in Nicaragua (Congress had banned this funding via the Boland Amendment)
- **Israel** shipped the weapons to Iran; US resupplied Israel
- **Iran** promised to work for hostage release
- **Lieutenant Colonel Oliver North** (National Security Council) devised the plan to **divert proceeds from Iranian arms sales to the Contras**

### The Scandal Breaks (November 1986)
- Lebanese magazine *Ash-Shiraa* exposed the arms sales
- Attorney General investigation revealed the Contra diversion
- Multiple congressional investigations and a **special counsel investigation** (Lawrence Walsh)

### Key Figures Convicted (later pardoned/overturned)
- **Oliver North**: Convicted of 3 felonies; conviction overturned on appeal
- **John Poindexter** (National Security Advisor): Convicted; overturned on appeal
- **Caspar Weinberger** (Secretary of Defense): Indicted; pardoned by George H.W. Bush (Christmas Eve 1992) before trial

### Reagan's Role
- Reagan was a **supporter of the Contra cause** but **maintained "plausible deniability"**
- Claimed in testimony he didn't remember or didn't know about the diversion
- Approval rating temporarily dropped but he survived politically
- Never fully resolved whether Reagan personally approved the Contra diversion

### Significance
- Showed the Reagan administration was willing to **violate federal law** (Boland Amendment) and **sell arms to a designated enemy state** (Iran) to pursue anti-communist goals
- Undermined the "law and order" conservative image
- Congressional findings: administration had created a "**shadow government**" operating outside normal oversight
- Contributed to later cynicism about government accountability

## The End of the Cold War

### Why the USSR Changed
- **Huge economic problems in the USSR**: stagnant growth, outdated industry, chronic shortages of consumer goods
- **War in Afghanistan** drained the Soviet economy (1979-1989) — "Soviet Vietnam"
- **Military spending** consumed unsustainable share of Soviet economy (estimated 15-20% of GDP vs. 6-7% for US)
- **Chernobyl disaster** (April 1986) exposed regime's dysfunction and lies
- **Oil prices crashed** (1986) — Saudi Arabia flooded market at Reagan's request; Soviet oil revenues collapsed

### Mikhail Gorbachev (Soviet leader 1985-1991)
Came to power March 1985, introduced two signature reforms:
- **Perestroika** ("restructuring"): Economic reform — attempted to introduce some market mechanisms while preserving socialism
- **Glasnost** ("openness"): Political reform — greater freedom of speech and press, reduced censorship
- Also pursued **arms control** with US and **Sinatra Doctrine** (let Eastern European countries "do it their way")

### Reagan-Gorbachev Summits
- **Geneva (November 1985)**: First meeting; established personal rapport
- **Reykjavik (October 1986)**: Nearly agreed to eliminate all nuclear weapons; broke down over SDI
- **Washington (December 1987)**: Signed **INF Treaty** (Intermediate-Range Nuclear Forces) — eliminated entire class of nuclear missiles
- **Moscow (May/June 1988)**: Reagan in Red Square, asked if he still considered USSR "the evil empire" — replied "No, that was another time, another era"

### "Mr. Gorbachev, Tear Down This Wall" (June 12, 1987)
- Reagan speech at Brandenburg Gate in West Berlin
- Challenged Gorbachev directly:
  > "Mr. Gorbachev, open this gate. Mr. Gorbachev, tear down this wall!"
- Speechwriters (State Department, NSC) had argued against the line as too provocative; Reagan insisted on keeping it
- 2 years later the wall did fall

### Berlin Wall Falls (November 9, 1989)
- East German government announced (mistakenly) that borders were open
- Crowds gathered at checkpoints, guards didn't know what to do
- East Germans flooded into West Berlin
- People climbed on wall, chipped pieces off with hammers
- **George H.W. Bush** was president by this point (inaugurated January 1989)
- East and West Germany reunified (October 3, 1990)

### Eastern Europe Falls (1989)
- **Poland**: Solidarity won semi-free elections (June 1989)
- **Hungary**: Opened border with Austria (May 1989)
- **Czechoslovakia**: "Velvet Revolution" (November 1989)
- **Romania**: Ceausescu overthrown and executed (December 1989)
- **Bulgaria**, **East Germany**: communist governments collapsed

### Soviet Union Dissolves (December 25, 1991)
- August 1991: hardline coup attempt against Gorbachev failed
- **Boris Yeltsin** (president of Russia) emerged as dominant figure
- December 8: leaders of Russia, Ukraine, Belarus declared USSR dissolved
- December 25: Gorbachev resigned, Soviet flag lowered over the Kremlin
- 15 republics became independent nations
- **Cold War officially over**

### Did Reagan "Win" the Cold War?
Debated among historians:
- **Reagan credit argument**: Military buildup + SDI + moral clarity ("Evil Empire") + support for Eastern European dissidents (Solidarity, etc.) bankrupted and delegitimized the USSR
- **Skeptical argument**: USSR was already in terminal decline before Reagan; Gorbachev's reforms were the proximate cause; Reagan's arms buildup just happened to coincide with Soviet collapse
- **Middle view**: Reagan didn't single-handedly win but his policies accelerated inevitable Soviet collapse; Gorbachev's reforms accelerated from within; both were necessary

## Reagan's Legacy

### Transformative Impact
- Shifted the political spectrum rightward
- Tax cuts for top earners became permanent GOP orthodoxy
- Military spending as economic stimulus became standard
- Deregulation and privatization became bipartisan (Clinton continued much of this)
- Religious right became permanent fixture of GOP politics
- Federal judiciary transformed — Reagan appointed ~half of federal judges including 3 Supreme Court justices (O'Connor, Scalia, Kennedy, plus elevating Rehnquist to Chief)

### Controversies and Failures
- **AIDS crisis**: Reagan did not publicly mention AIDS until **1985** (4 years into the epidemic); administration treated it as a gay/drug issue, not a public health crisis. Tens of thousands died with minimal federal response
- **Homelessness**: Skyrocketed under Reagan
- **Iran-Contra**: Major scandal, avoided impeachment
- **Environment**: Gutted EPA, opened public lands to resource extraction
- **Labor**: Broke **PATCO** (air traffic controllers) strike, 1981 — fired 11,000 striking workers, effectively ending era of strong private-sector unionization
- **National debt**: Tripled
- **Inequality**: Widened dramatically
- **Mass incarceration**: Exploded
- **Latin America death tolls**: Hundreds of thousands killed in Central American wars

### Political Legacy
- Every Republican since has claimed to be "Reagan's heir"
- Democrats adapted — **Clinton (1992)**: "The era of big government is over"
- Reagan's basic philosophical frame — government is the problem — dominated American politics for 30+ years, arguably until Obama (2008) or even 2016
