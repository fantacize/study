## 1. USSR Detonates an Atomic Bomb (August 29, 1949)

### What Happened
- The Soviet Union successfully tested its first nuclear weapon, **"First Lightning"** (codenamed **Joe-1** by Americans, after Joseph Stalin), at the **Semipalatinsk Test Site** in Kazakhstan
- The bomb was a plutonium implosion device, nearly identical in design to the American **"Fat Man"** bomb dropped on Nagasaki
- US intelligence detected the test on **September 3, 1949** when a WB-29 weather reconnaissance aircraft collected radioactive debris over the North Pacific
- President Truman publicly announced the Soviet test on **September 23, 1949**

### Why It Happened So Fast
- American scientists had predicted the USSR wouldn't develop a bomb until **1953-1954**
- Several factors accelerated the Soviet program:
  - **Klaus Fuchs**: German-born British physicist who worked on the Manhattan Project at Los Alamos. He was a Soviet spy who passed detailed bomb designs to Moscow. Arrested in 1950 and sentenced to 14 years in a British prison
  - **Soviet scientific talent**: Physicists **Igor Kurchatov** (led the program), **Andrei Sakharov** (later developed the hydrogen bomb), and others were world-class scientists
  - **Stalin's priority**: After Hiroshima, Stalin made the bomb program the USSR's highest priority, devoting massive resources and using gulag labor to mine uranium
  - The Soviets had been working on nuclear research since 1943

### Immediate Consequences
1. **Ended America's nuclear monopoly**: The US had been the sole nuclear power since 1945. That strategic advantage vanished overnight
2. **Hydrogen bomb development**: Truman approved the crash development of the **thermonuclear (hydrogen) bomb** in January 1950 - a weapon **1,000 times more powerful** than the Hiroshima bomb. The US tested its first H-bomb ("Ivy Mike") on November 1, 1952; the Soviets followed with theirs in August 1953
3. **Nuclear arms race**: Both nations entered a spiraling competition to build more and bigger weapons. By the 1960s, both had enough nuclear weapons to destroy civilization multiple times over (**overkill**)
4. **Domestic fear**: Combined with the "loss" of China (October 1949), the Soviet bomb created near-panic in the US. It seemed like communism was winning everywhere
5. **Spy hysteria**: How did the Soviets get the bomb so fast? Espionage. This fueled the Red Scare, McCarthyism, and the Rosenberg trial
6. **NSC-68**: The Soviet bomb was a key factor behind NSC-68 (April 1950), which called for massive military buildup

### Significance
The Soviet atomic test transformed the Cold War from a political and economic competition into an **existential nuclear standoff**. For the next 40 years, humanity lived under the threat of nuclear annihilation. The doctrine of **Mutual Assured Destruction (MAD)** - the idea that neither side would attack because both would be destroyed - became the terrifying foundation of Cold War "peace." Duck and Cover drills, bomb shelters, and nuclear anxiety became defining features of American life.

## 2. The Berlin Airlift (June 24, 1948 - May 12, 1949)

### Background: Why Berlin Was a Problem
- After WWII, Germany was divided into **four occupation zones**: American, British, French, and Soviet
- **Berlin**, the capital, was located **110 miles inside the Soviet zone** but was also divided into four sectors
- West Berlin (American, British, French sectors) was a capitalist island surrounded by Soviet-controlled territory
- In 1948, the Western allies introduced a new currency (**Deutschmark**) in their zones to stabilize the economy. Stalin saw this as a move to create a permanent Western-aligned West Germany

### The Blockade
- **June 24, 1948**: Stalin ordered all road, rail, and canal access to West Berlin **closed**
- 2.5 million West Berliners were cut off from food, fuel, medicine, and all supplies
- Stalin's goal: Force the Western allies to either:
  - Abandon Berlin entirely, OR
  - Accept Soviet currency and control over all of Berlin
- Stalin believed the West would give in rather than risk war

### The Airlift Response: Operation Vittles
- Truman refused to abandon Berlin but also refused to send armed convoys (which could trigger war)
- Instead: **fly everything in by air**
- The airlift was initially seen as impossible - West Berlin needed **4,500 tons of supplies PER DAY** (food, coal for heating and power generation, raw materials)
- **The logistics were staggering**:
  - At peak efficiency: a plane landed at Berlin's Tempelhof Airport every **30 seconds**, 24 hours a day
  - Used three 20-mile-wide air corridors that the Soviets had agreed to keep open
  - **277,000+ flights** over 11 months
  - Delivered over **2.3 million tons** of cargo
  - Coal was the biggest challenge - Berlin needed 1,500 tons daily just for heating and electricity
  - Planes sometimes carried candy, earning pilot **Gail Halvorsen** the nickname **"The Candy Bomber"** - he attached chocolate bars and gum to miniature parachutes made of handkerchiefs and dropped them to children watching the planes land
- British and American pilots flew in terrible weather conditions; **101 people died** in accidents during the airlift
- **Cost**: Approximately $224 million (over $2.5 billion today)

### The Soviet Response
- Stalin did NOT shoot down the planes - doing so would mean war
- He had gambled that the airlift would fail and the West would negotiate
- As the airlift succeeded month after month, it became a massive propaganda humiliation for the USSR
- **May 12, 1949**: Stalin quietly lifted the blockade, having achieved nothing

### Consequences
1. **NATO created** (April 4, 1949): The crisis convinced Western nations they needed a formal military alliance
2. **Germany permanently divided**:
   - **Federal Republic of Germany (West Germany)** established May 1949
   - **German Democratic Republic (East Germany)** established October 1949
3. **West Berlin became a symbol** of Western resolve and freedom
4. **Template for Cold War**: Confrontation short of direct war; victory through determination, logistics, and public opinion rather than combat

### Significance
The Berlin Airlift was the **first major direct confrontation** of the Cold War and established critical precedents. It showed that the US would defend its interests but would seek creative alternatives to war. The Soviets learned that blockades and pressure tactics could backfire spectacularly. The airlift also demonstrated the power of **soft power** - the image of Americans feeding starving Berliners was more effective than any propaganda campaign.

## 3. The Start of the Korean War (June 25, 1950)

### Background: How Korea Was Divided
- Korea had been a Japanese colony from 1910-1945
- After Japan's surrender, Korea was divided at the **38th parallel**:
  - **North Korea** (Democratic People's Republic of Korea): Occupied by the Soviet Union, led by **Kim Il-sung**, a communist guerrilla fighter who had served in the Soviet army
  - **South Korea** (Republic of Korea): Occupied by the United States, led by **Syngman Rhee**, an anti-communist authoritarian who had lived in the US for decades
- Both leaders claimed to be the legitimate government of ALL Korea
- Both the US and USSR withdrew their troops by 1949, leaving behind armed and hostile Korean governments

### The Invasion
- **June 25, 1950**: 75,000 North Korean troops, equipped with Soviet **T-34 tanks**, stormed across the 38th parallel in a surprise invasion
- The South Korean army was poorly equipped and quickly overwhelmed
- Within **three days**, North Korean forces captured **Seoul**, the South Korean capital
- **Stalin had approved the invasion** after Kim Il-sung convinced him the US would not intervene and that South Koreans would welcome liberation
- **Mao Zedong** (China) also gave his blessing

### The US Response
- Truman immediately committed US forces under the authority of the **United Nations** (the USSR was boycotting the Security Council over the refusal to seat Communist China, so they couldn't veto the resolution)
- Truman called it a **"police action"**, not a war - avoiding a formal declaration of war from Congress
- Initially, US and South Korean forces were pushed into a tiny perimeter around **Pusan** in the southeast corner of Korea

### Key Phases of the War
1. **Inchon Landing (September 15, 1950)**: General **Douglas MacArthur** executed a brilliant amphibious landing at Inchon, behind North Korean lines. Cut off and surrounded, the North Korean army collapsed. UN forces recaptured Seoul and pushed north
2. **Drive to the Yalu River (October-November 1950)**: MacArthur pushed north toward the Chinese border (Yalu River), ignoring warnings that China would intervene
3. **Chinese Intervention (November 1950)**: **300,000 Chinese "volunteers"** (regular army troops) poured across the Yalu. Caught by surprise in brutal winter conditions, UN forces suffered one of the worst retreats in US military history (the **Chosin Reservoir** retreat - temperatures reached -35°F)
4. **Stalemate (1951-1953)**: The front line stabilized near the 38th parallel. Two more years of bloody fighting for small patches of ground. Truce talks began in July 1951 but dragged on for two years

### MacArthur vs. Truman
- MacArthur publicly demanded permission to bomb China, blockade the Chinese coast, and even use **nuclear weapons**
- Truman refused - expanding the war risked bringing the Soviet Union in and starting WWIII
- MacArthur publicly criticized Truman's strategy, violating the principle of civilian control of the military
- **April 11, 1951**: Truman **fired MacArthur** - an enormously unpopular decision (MacArthur was a national hero). MacArthur returned to a ticker-tape parade and addressed Congress ("Old soldiers never die, they just fade away")
- **Significance of the firing**: Preserved the principle that elected civilian leaders, not generals, make war policy. Established the precedent of **limited war** - fighting with restraint to avoid nuclear escalation

### The Armistice (July 27, 1953)
- Signed after Eisenhower took office and hinted at using nuclear weapons if talks failed
- Established the **Korean Demilitarized Zone (DMZ)** at roughly the 38th parallel - almost exactly where the war started
- **No peace treaty was ever signed** - technically, North and South Korea are still at war today
- Created the **Military Armistice Commission** to oversee the ceasefire

### The Human Cost
| Category | Estimate |
|----------|----------|
| American dead | ~36,574 |
| American wounded | ~103,000 |
| South Korean military dead | ~137,000 |
| North Korean military dead | ~215,000 |
| Chinese military dead | ~400,000+ |
| Korean civilian dead (both sides) | ~2-3 million |
| Refugees | ~5 million |

### Significance
- **First "hot" war of the Cold War**: Proved that the Cold War could turn into real, devastating combat
- **Established limited war**: Fighting without using nuclear weapons and without seeking total victory. This was a new concept in American military thinking
- **Containment tested**: The US successfully prevented communist takeover of South Korea, validating the containment strategy
- **Set precedent for Vietnam**: The idea that the US could fight limited wars to contain communism in Asia led directly to involvement in Vietnam
- **Forgotten War**: Despite its enormous cost, Korea is often overshadowed by WWII and Vietnam in American memory
- **Korea remains divided today**: The DMZ is the most heavily militarized border in the world. North Korea developed nuclear weapons. The conflict's legacy continues

## 4. The Marshall Plan (1948-1952)

### Background: Europe in Ruins
- WWII devastated Europe:
  - **Cities bombed to rubble**: London, Berlin, Dresden, Warsaw, Rotterdam, Stalingrad
  - **Infrastructure destroyed**: roads, bridges, railroads, ports, factories
  - **40 million Europeans dead** (military and civilian)
  - **Millions displaced**: refugees, prisoners of war, concentration camp survivors wandering across the continent
  - **Economies shattered**: factories destroyed, trade networks broken, currencies worthless
  - **Harvests failed**: the brutal winter of 1946-47 was one of the worst in European history
  - **Starvation**: food rations in some areas were below concentration camp levels
- By 1947, Europeans were desperate and hungry - conditions that historically breed extremism

### The Strategic Fear
- Communist parties were **surging** in Western Europe:
  - **France**: Communist Party won 28.6% of the vote in 1946 - the largest single party
  - **Italy**: Communist Party was the second-largest political party
  - **Greece**: Communist guerrillas were fighting a civil war against the government
- American policymakers feared that if Western Europe's economies collapsed, voters would turn to communism - not through Soviet invasion but through **democratic elections**
- As Truman said: "The seeds of totalitarian regimes are nurtured by misery and want"

### The Plan
- **June 5, 1947**: Secretary of State **George C. Marshall** proposed massive American economic aid in his Harvard commencement address
- Officially called the **European Recovery Program (ERP)**
- Key features:
  - **$13.3 billion** in aid over 4 years (approximately **$150 billion** in today's dollars)
  - Open to **ALL European nations**, including the Soviet Union and Eastern Bloc
  - Required recipient nations to cooperate with each other and reduce trade barriers
  - Mixed grants and loans for food, fuel, raw materials, machinery, and industrial equipment
  - Recipients had to create national recovery plans and account for how money was spent

### Who Got What (Largest Recipients)
| Country | Amount (millions) |
|---------|------------------|
| United Kingdom | $3,190 |
| France | $2,714 |
| Italy | $1,509 |
| West Germany | $1,391 |
| Netherlands | $1,083 |
| Greece | $706 |
| Austria | $678 |

### Stalin's Response
- The offer was deliberately extended to the USSR and Eastern Europe
- **Stalin refused** and **forbade** Eastern Bloc countries from participating
- He viewed the Marshall Plan as:
  - American economic imperialism - creating dependent client states
  - A strategy to rebuild Germany (which had just devastated the USSR)
  - A tool to undermine Soviet influence in Eastern Europe
- Created the **Molotov Plan** (1947) and later **COMECON** (Council for Mutual Economic Assistance, 1949) as the Soviet alternative - but these never matched Marshall Plan effectiveness
- **Czechoslovakia** initially accepted the invitation but Stalin forced them to reverse the decision - this heavy-handed intervention pushed many Czech democrats toward opposing the Soviets, contributing to the communist coup of February 1948

### Results
- **Industrial production** in Marshall Plan countries increased **35%** above pre-war levels by 1952
- **GDP** of recipient countries grew between **15-25%** during the program
- **Trade** between Western European nations increased dramatically
- Communist parties in France and Italy lost support as economies improved
- Created the foundation for **European integration** - the plan required cooperation, which eventually led to the European Coal and Steel Community (1951) and ultimately the European Union
- Rebuilt Germany as a prosperous, democratic Western ally

### Significance
The Marshall Plan is widely considered the **most successful foreign policy initiative in American history**. It demonstrated that:
- Economic weapons could be as powerful as military ones in the Cold War
- Rebuilding former enemies (Germany) rather than punishing them (as after WWI) produced better outcomes
- American prosperity depended on a prosperous world trading system
- The US had to choose between its isolationist traditions and global leadership - it chose leadership
- **Contrast with the Soviet approach**: While the US rebuilt Western Europe, the USSR stripped Eastern Europe of industrial equipment, imposed reparations, and subordinated Eastern economies to Soviet needs. The divergence in living standards between West and East Europe over the next four decades was stark evidence of which system worked better

## 5. China Becomes Communist (October 1, 1949)

### Background: The Chinese Civil War
- China had been in turmoil for decades:
  - **1911**: Qing Dynasty (imperial China) collapsed
  - **1920s-1940s**: Civil war between **Nationalists (Kuomintang/KMT)** led by **Chiang Kai-shek** and **Communists (CCP)** led by **Mao Zedong**
  - WWII interrupted the civil war as both sides fought the Japanese invasion (1937-1945), but they never truly cooperated
  - After Japan's surrender (1945), the civil war resumed immediately

### Why the Communists Won
- **Chiang Kai-shek's Nationalists** had serious problems:
  - Massive corruption - US aid was stolen or wasted by KMT officials
  - Incompetent military leadership
  - Hyperinflation destroyed the economy (prices doubled every few days)
  - Alienated peasants through land policies that favored wealthy landlords
  - The US provided **$2 billion** in aid but it wasn't enough to compensate for internal rot
- **Mao Zedong's Communists** had key advantages:
  - **Land reform**: Promised (and delivered) land redistribution to peasants - this won massive rural support in a country that was 85% peasant
  - **Disciplined army**: The People's Liberation Army (PLA) was well-organized and motivated
  - **Guerrilla tactics**: Mao literally wrote the book on guerrilla warfare
  - **Soviet support**: Military equipment, especially from captured Japanese stockpiles in Manchuria
  - **Popular legitimacy**: Many Chinese saw the Communists as less corrupt and more committed to ordinary people

### The Communist Victory
- By 1949, the Nationalist position collapsed rapidly
- Major cities fell one after another: Manchuria (1948), Beijing (January 1949), Nanjing (April 1949), Shanghai (May 1949)
- **October 1, 1949**: Mao Zedong stood on the Gate of Heavenly Peace (Tiananmen) in Beijing and proclaimed the **People's Republic of China (PRC)**
  - Famous declaration: "The Chinese people have stood up!"
- Chiang Kai-shek and ~2 million Nationalists fled to the island of **Taiwan** (Formosa), where they established the **Republic of China**
- The US continued to recognize Taiwan as the "real" China until 1979

### American Reaction: "Who Lost China?"
- The "loss" of China sent shockwaves through American politics
- **"Who lost China?"** became a bitter political question:
  - Republicans blamed the Truman administration for not doing enough to save Chiang
  - The **China Lobby** (pro-Nationalist politicians and journalists) accused the State Department of harboring communist sympathizers
  - State Department **China experts** who had accurately predicted Chiang's defeat were purged - accused of being disloyal because their analysis proved correct
  - This purge removed America's best Asia experts at precisely the moment they were needed most (contributing to later mistakes in Korea and Vietnam)
- Combined with the Soviet atomic bomb (August 1949), it seemed like communism was advancing on all fronts

### Consequences
1. **800 million people** under communist rule (1/4 of world's population) - the communist world now stretched from East Berlin to the Pacific
2. **Sino-Soviet Alliance** (February 1950): China and USSR signed a mutual defense treaty - the West faced a seemingly monolithic communist bloc
3. **Korean War**: Mao's China intervened in Korea (November 1950), sending 300,000 troops
4. **McCarthyism fueled**: "Who lost China?" hysteria strengthened McCarthy's hand. If China could fall, communists must be everywhere
5. **US-Taiwan alliance**: The US committed to defending Taiwan, a commitment that continues today
6. **Two-China problem**: For 30 years, the US refused to recognize the PRC, pretending 700 million people didn't exist diplomatically

### What Happened in Communist China
- **Land Reform (1949-1953)**: Redistributed land from landlords to peasants; estimated **1-2 million landlords killed** in the process
- **Korean War (1950-53)**: 400,000+ Chinese soldiers killed
- **Great Leap Forward (1958-1962)**: Mao's disastrous industrialization campaign caused the **worst famine in human history** - estimated **30-45 million dead**
- **Cultural Revolution (1966-1976)**: Mao unleashed radical youth ("Red Guards") against perceived enemies; millions persecuted, tortured, killed; cultural heritage destroyed

### Significance
The "loss" of China fundamentally shaped American Cold War policy. It created the political environment where no president could afford to "lose" another country to communism - this fear drove intervention in Korea, Vietnam, and dozens of other Cold War conflicts. The idea that communism was a monolithic, expanding force (rather than a collection of different national movements) became an article of faith in American foreign policy, leading to strategic mistakes that lasted decades.

## 6. The Truman Doctrine (March 12, 1947)

### Background: Crisis in Greece and Turkey
- **Greece**: Since 1946, communist guerrillas (backed by Yugoslavia, Albania, and Bulgaria) had been fighting the Greek government in a civil war. The British had been supporting the Greek government but announced in February 1947 that they could no longer afford to do so - Britain was broke
- **Turkey**: The Soviet Union was pressuring Turkey for control of the **Dardanelles** (the strategic strait connecting the Black Sea to the Mediterranean) and for territorial concessions in eastern Turkey. The Soviets also kept troops massed on the Turkish border
- If Greece fell to communism, Turkey would be isolated, and Soviet influence would extend into the eastern Mediterranean - threatening oil routes and the Suez Canal

### Truman's Speech to Congress (March 12, 1947)
- Requested **$400 million** in military and economic aid for Greece ($300M) and Turkey ($100M)
- Framed the issue in sweeping, universal terms - not just about two countries but about the entire free world

### Key Quotes
> "At the present moment in world history nearly every nation must choose between alternative ways of life. The choice is too often not a free one."

> "One way of life is based upon the will of the majority... free institutions, representative government, free elections, guarantees of individual liberty, freedom of speech and religion, and freedom from political oppression."

> "The second way of life is based upon the will of a minority forcibly imposed upon the majority. It relies upon terror and oppression, a controlled press and radio, fixed elections, and the suppression of personal freedoms."

> "I believe that it must be the policy of the United States to support free peoples who are resisting attempted subjugation by armed minorities or by outside pressures."

> "The seeds of totalitarian regimes are nurtured by misery and want. They spread and grow in the evil soil of poverty and strife."

### Debate and Passage
- **Senator Arthur Vandenberg** (Republican, former isolationist) supported the doctrine, saying Truman needed to "scare the hell out of the American people" to get Congress to fund it
- Some critics argued:
  - The Greek government was itself authoritarian and corrupt
  - The US was committing itself to an open-ended, global obligation
  - This bypassed the United Nations
- Congress approved the aid in May 1947
- **Result in Greece**: Communist insurgency defeated by 1949 (also helped by Yugoslavia's split from the Soviet bloc)
- **Result in Turkey**: Remained a Western ally, joined NATO in 1952

### The Doctrine's Broader Implications
- **End of isolationism**: The US committed to permanent, global engagement against communism
- **Open-ended commitment**: "Support free peoples... anywhere" meant the US could intervene in any country facing communist pressure - from Western Europe to Southeast Asia to Latin America
- **Binary framework**: Reduced complex political situations to a choice between "freedom" and "totalitarianism" - this oversimplification led to supporting authoritarian regimes simply because they were anti-communist (Shah of Iran, Batista in Cuba, Pinochet in Chile, apartheid South Africa)
- **Precedent for everything that followed**: Korea, Vietnam, covert operations in Iran, Guatemala, Chile, and dozens of other interventions were justified by the logic of the Truman Doctrine

### Significance
The Truman Doctrine was the **official declaration of the Cold War** from the American side. It committed the United States to a global strategy of containment that lasted 44 years, cost trillions of dollars, and shaped every aspect of American foreign and domestic policy. It was simultaneously:
- A genuine response to a real threat (Soviet expansion in Europe)
- A moral crusade (freedom vs. tyranny)
- A strategic overreach (committing to defend "free peoples" everywhere without defining limits)
The tension between these three aspects defined American foreign policy for the rest of the century.

## 7. The Creation of NATO (April 4, 1949)

### Background: Why Europe Needed a Military Alliance
- By 1949, the Cold War had intensified dramatically:
  - **Communist coup in Czechoslovakia** (February 1948): The last democracy in Eastern Europe fell to communism - alarmed Western Europe
  - **Berlin Blockade** (June 1948 - May 1949): Demonstrated that the Soviets were willing to use aggressive tactics
  - **Soviet atomic bomb** (August 1949, tested months after NATO's founding): Raised the stakes enormously
- Western European nations were individually too weak to resist Soviet pressure
- The **Brussels Treaty** (March 1948) - a mutual defense pact between Britain, France, Belgium, Netherlands, and Luxembourg - was inadequate without American participation

### The Treaty
- Signed **April 4, 1949** in Washington, DC
- **Original 12 members**: United States, Canada, United Kingdom, France, Belgium, Netherlands, Luxembourg, Norway, Denmark, Iceland, Italy, Portugal
- Later additions: Greece and Turkey (1952), West Germany (1955), Spain (1982)

### Article 5: The Core Commitment
> "The Parties agree that an armed attack against one or more of them in Europe or in North America shall be considered as an attack against them all."

- This was revolutionary: the first **permanent peacetime military alliance** in American history
- Broke with 150 years of tradition since George Washington warned against "entangling alliances"
- Required Senate ratification - passed **82-13** (July 21, 1949)
- **Senator Robert Taft** (Ohio Republican) led the opposition, arguing it would provoke the Soviets and commit the US to defending countries it couldn't actually protect

### Lord Ismay's Famous Summary
NATO's first Secretary General, **Lord Hastings Ismay**, described NATO's three purposes:
- **"To keep the Russians out"** - deter Soviet aggression
- **"The Americans in"** - ensure the US stayed committed to European defense
- **"The Germans down"** - prevent German remilitarization from threatening Europe again

### How NATO Worked in Practice
- **Supreme Allied Commander Europe (SACEUR)**: Always an American general (first was Eisenhower, 1951)
- **Nuclear umbrella**: The US extended its nuclear deterrent to cover all NATO allies. An attack on any NATO country would be treated as an attack on the US, including a potential nuclear response
- **Forward defense**: NATO troops (especially American) stationed in West Germany, ready to resist a Soviet invasion
- **Standardized military procedures**: NATO allies trained together, shared intelligence, and standardized weapons and communications
- **Political dimension**: Regular consultations between member governments; decisions made by consensus

### Soviet Response: The Warsaw Pact (May 14, 1955)
- Created after West Germany joined NATO (a direct provocation from the Soviet perspective)
- Members: USSR, Poland, East Germany, Czechoslovakia, Hungary, Romania, Bulgaria, Albania (withdrew 1968)
- On paper, similar to NATO. In practice, completely dominated by the Soviet Union
- The USSR used the Warsaw Pact to justify military interventions against its own allies:
  - **Hungary (1956)**: Soviet tanks crushed the Hungarian Revolution, killing ~2,500 Hungarians
  - **Czechoslovakia (1968)**: Warsaw Pact forces invaded to crush the "Prague Spring" reforms
- **Significance**: The Warsaw Pact formalized Soviet control over Eastern Europe and ensured that any Eastern European attempt at independence could be suppressed by collective military force

### Significance of NATO
- **Deterrence worked**: The Soviet Union never attacked a NATO member. Whether this was because of NATO or because the Soviets never intended to attack is debated, but the correlation is clear
- **Locked in the Cold War division**: Europe was formally split into two armed camps. The "Iron Curtain" became a military reality
- **American global commitment**: NATO made the US the guarantor of European security permanently. American troops are still stationed in Europe today
- **Survived the Cold War**: Unlike the Warsaw Pact (dissolved 1991), NATO expanded after the Cold War, incorporating former Soviet allies (Poland, Czech Republic, Hungary joined 1999; Baltic states joined 2004). This expansion remains deeply controversial
- **Article 5 invoked only once**: After the September 11, 2001 attacks - NATO allies deployed to Afghanistan alongside the US

## 8. HUAC and the Hollywood Ten (1947)

### Background: The House Un-American Activities Committee (HUAC)
- **HUAC** was a committee of the US House of Representatives, created in **1938** to investigate "subversive" activities
- Originally focused on both fascist and communist threats; after WWII, focused exclusively on communism
- HUAC had the power to **subpoena witnesses** (compel them to testify), hold public hearings, and recommend contempt charges for those who refused to cooperate
- The committee's real power was **publicity** - being called before HUAC, even without being charged with any crime, could destroy a career through association and innuendo

### The Hollywood Investigation (October 1947)
- HUAC turned its attention to the **film industry**, arguing that communists had infiltrated Hollywood and were inserting communist propaganda into American movies
- **"Friendly" witnesses** testified first:
  - **Walt Disney** claimed communist agitators had instigated a 1941 animators' strike at his studio
  - **Ronald Reagan** (then president of the Screen Actors Guild) testified that communists had tried to influence the guild but that the industry could police itself
  - **Gary Cooper**, **Robert Taylor**, and other stars cooperated
  - Studio head **Jack Warner** (Warner Bros.) named suspected communists to protect his studio

### The Hollywood Ten
Ten writers and directors **refused to cooperate** with HUAC, invoking the **First Amendment** (freedom of speech and association) rather than the Fifth Amendment (self-incrimination):

1. **Alvah Bessie** - screenwriter
2. **Herbert Biberman** - director
3. **Lester Cole** - screenwriter
4. **Edward Dmytryk** - director (*Crossfire*, 1947 - a film about antisemitism)
5. **Ring Lardner Jr.** - screenwriter (*Woman of the Year*, 1942)
6. **John Howard Lawson** - screenwriter, head of the Communist Party's Hollywood branch
7. **Albert Maltz** - screenwriter
8. **Samuel Ornitz** - screenwriter
9. **Adrian Scott** - producer (*Crossfire*)
10. **Dalton Trumbo** - screenwriter (*Roman Holiday*, *Spartacus*, *Johnny Got His Gun*)

### What Happened at the Hearings
- The Ten were combative and defiant. When asked "Are you now or have you ever been a member of the Communist Party?" they:
  - Demanded to read prepared statements (denied by committee chairman **J. Parnell Thomas**)
  - Argued the question violated their First Amendment rights
  - Were gaveled into silence and removed from the hearing room
- **John Howard Lawson** shouted: "I am not on trial here... This committee is on trial here before the American people!"
- **Dalton Trumbo** declared: "This is the beginning of an American concentration camp"

### The Consequences
- All ten were found in **contempt of Congress**
- Each was sentenced to **6-12 months in federal prison** and fined $1,000
- All were **fired** by their studios immediately

### The Blacklist
- On **November 25, 1947**, the **Waldorf Statement** was issued by 48 film executives (meeting at the Waldorf-Astoria Hotel in New York):
  - Fired or suspended the Hollywood Ten
  - Declared that no communist or anyone refusing to cooperate with Congress would be employed in the industry
  - This created the **Hollywood Blacklist** - an informal but devastating ban on employment
- The blacklist eventually included **over 300 people**: actors, writers, directors, musicians, and other entertainment professionals
- Careers were destroyed based on:
  - Actual Communist Party membership
  - Attending a meeting years ago
  - Signing a petition
  - Being named by someone else trying to save their own career
  - Association with suspected communists
- Some blacklisted writers worked under **pseudonyms** or used **fronts** (other writers who put their names on the work):
  - **Dalton Trumbo** won an Oscar for *The Brave One* (1956) under the name "Robert Rich" - no one collected the award
  - He also wrote *Roman Holiday* (1953) under a front's name; the Academy posthumously corrected the credit in 2011
- The blacklist lasted roughly from **1947 to 1960**. It began to crumble when:
  - **Kirk Douglas** publicly credited Trumbo as the screenwriter of *Spartacus* (1960)
  - **Otto Preminger** credited Trumbo for *Exodus* (1960)
  - President-elect **John F. Kennedy** crossed a picket line to see *Spartacus*, effectively legitimizing the film

### Ironic Footnotes
- Committee chairman **J. Parnell Thomas** was himself convicted of **fraud** (padding his congressional payroll) and sent to the same federal prison as two of the Hollywood Ten
- **Edward Dmytryk** eventually cooperated with HUAC, naming 26 colleagues. He was the only one of the Ten to do so - and was the only one whose career survived relatively intact. The others never forgave him

### Significance
The Hollywood Ten case established the precedent for political persecution during the Red Scare. It demonstrated that:
- **Constitutional rights could be overridden** by fear: The First Amendment did not protect the Ten from contempt charges
- **Guilt by association** could destroy lives without criminal charges or trials
- **Economic coercion** (the blacklist) was as effective as government action in suppressing dissent
- **Fear was self-reinforcing**: Once people saw what happened to the Ten, most chose to cooperate rather than resist, which expanded the list of accused and perpetuated the cycle
- The Hollywood Blacklist remains one of the most significant episodes of political repression in American history, and a cautionary tale about how democratic societies can abandon their own principles in times of fear

## 9. The Execution of Julius and Ethel Rosenberg (June 19, 1953)

### Background: The Spy Ring
- In 1950, the FBI uncovered a Soviet espionage network that had stolen **atomic bomb secrets** from the Manhattan Project
- The trail began with **Klaus Fuchs** (arrested in Britain, February 1950), who confessed to spying and named his American contact: **Harry Gold**
- Gold named **David Greenglass**, a machinist who had worked at the Los Alamos weapons laboratory
- Greenglass named his sister and brother-in-law: **Ethel** and **Julius Rosenberg**

### Julius Rosenberg (1918-1953)
- Electrical engineer, City College of New York graduate
- Recruited by Soviet intelligence in the early 1940s
- Ran a spy ring that collected military and industrial secrets
- Recruited David Greenglass (his brother-in-law) to steal information from Los Alamos
- Also passed information about radar, sonar, and jet engines to the Soviets
- **Evidence against him was strong**: Multiple witnesses, corroborating testimony, and (later revealed) decoded Soviet communications (**Venona intercepts**) confirmed his espionage activities

### Ethel Rosenberg (1915-1953)
- Julius's wife, mother of two young sons (**Michael**, age 10, and **Robert**, age 6, at the time of execution)
- The case against her was **much weaker**:
  - The primary evidence was David Greenglass's testimony that Ethel typed up his handwritten notes about the bomb. This made her part of the conspiracy
  - **In 2001, Greenglass admitted he had lied** about Ethel's involvement to protect his own wife (Ruth Greenglass, who actually did the typing). He told a reporter: "I don't know who typed it, frankly, and to this day I can't remember that the typing took place... I frankly think my wife did the typing, but I don't remember"
  - Prosecutors likely charged Ethel as **leverage** to pressure Julius into confessing and naming other spies. He never did
  - The Venona intercepts (declassified in the 1990s) confirmed Julius's extensive espionage but showed Ethel's role was minimal - she was aware of Julius's activities but was not an active spy

### The Trial (March 6-29, 1951)
- Tried in **New York City** before Judge **Irving Kaufman**
- Key prosecution witnesses:
  - **David Greenglass**: Ethel's own brother testified against her
  - **Harry Gold**: Admitted courier between Fuchs and the spy ring
  - **Elizabeth Bentley**: Former Soviet spy who had become an FBI informant
- The Rosenbergs **maintained their innocence** throughout, invoking the **Fifth Amendment** when asked about Communist Party membership
- **Jury convicted both** on March 29, 1951 after deliberating for less than a day

### The Sentence
- Judge Kaufman sentenced both to **death** on April 5, 1951
- His statement was extraordinary:
  > "I consider your crime worse than murder... I believe your conduct in putting into the hands of the Russians the A-bomb years before our best scientists predicted Russia would perfect the bomb has already caused, in my opinion, the Communist aggression in Korea, with the resultant casualties exceeding 50,000 and who knows but that millions more of innocent people may pay the price of your treason."
- Kaufman blamed the Rosenbergs for the Korean War and potentially millions of future deaths
- This was **the first peacetime execution for espionage** in American history
- Ethel Rosenberg was **the first American woman executed by the federal government** since Mary Surratt (Lincoln assassination conspirator, 1865)

### Appeals and Protests
- The case became a **global cause celebre**:
  - **Pope Pius XII** appealed for clemency
  - **Albert Einstein** asked for a pardon
  - Mass protests in **Paris, London, and major cities worldwide**
  - Philosopher **Jean-Paul Sartre** called it "a legal lynching which smears with blood a whole nation"
  - Petition with **millions of signatures** demanding clemency
- President **Eisenhower refused clemency** twice, saying: "I can only say that, by immeasurably increasing the chances of atomic war, the Rosenbergs may have condemned to death tens of millions of innocent people all over the world"
- The Supreme Court briefly issued a stay of execution, which was overturned in a special session

### The Execution (June 19, 1953)
- Both were executed in the **electric chair** at **Sing Sing Prison** in Ossining, New York
- Julius was executed first, at 8:04 PM. He was pronounced dead after two shocks
- Ethel was executed at 8:11 PM. After three shocks, her heart was still beating. Two more shocks were applied before she was pronounced dead at 8:16 PM. The execution was widely described as gruesome
- Their sons, Michael (10) and Robert (6), were eventually adopted by the songwriter **Abel Meeropol** (who wrote "Strange Fruit," the anti-lynching song made famous by Billie Holiday)

### Was the Sentence Just? The Ongoing Debate

**Arguments that the sentence was too harsh:**
- Ethel's role was minimal - she was likely convicted to pressure Julius
- David Greenglass lied about Ethel's involvement (admitted in 2001)
- The information passed was not as critical as claimed - Soviet scientists were already close to developing the bomb independently
- Klaus Fuchs (who gave the Soviets far more valuable information) received only **14 years** in a British prison
- David Greenglass (who actually stole the secrets) received only **15 years** and was released in 1960
- Harry Gold received **30 years**
- The death sentence was disproportionate and influenced by Cold War hysteria
- Judge Kaufman consulted improperly with the prosecution before sentencing

**Arguments supporting the conviction:**
- Julius Rosenberg was genuinely a Soviet spy who caused serious damage to national security
- The Venona intercepts confirmed his extensive espionage network
- He had opportunities to cooperate and refused
- The sentence, while harsh, reflected the genuine national security threat

### What We Know Now (Post-Cold War Revelations)
- **Venona Project** (declassified 1995): Decoded Soviet communications confirmed Julius ran an extensive spy ring (codenamed "LIBERAL")
- **Soviet archives** (partially opened after 1991): Confirmed Julius's activities but showed Ethel was not a significant participant
- **Alexander Feklisov** (Julius's Soviet handler) confirmed in a 1997 interview that Julius provided valuable information but that it was "supplementary" to what the Soviets already had from Fuchs
- **David Greenglass's 2001 recantation**: Admitted he lied about Ethel typing the notes

### Significance
The Rosenberg case crystallizes the central tensions of the Cold War home front:
- **Real threats vs. overreaction**: Julius was a genuine spy, but the punishment far exceeded the crime, especially for Ethel
- **Justice vs. politics**: The trial and sentencing were deeply influenced by Cold War fear rather than proportional justice
- **Individual rights vs. national security**: How far should a democracy go in punishing those who threaten it?
- **The human cost of the Red Scare**: Two children lost both parents. Ethel Rosenberg almost certainly did not deserve to die. The case remains a powerful symbol of how fear can distort justice
- The Rosenberg case, alongside McCarthyism and the Hollywood Blacklist, represents the **domestic damage** the Cold War inflicted on American democracy itself

## Key Connections Across All Events

### Chronological Chain Reaction (1947-1953)
1. **Truman Doctrine** (March 1947) → Commits US to global containment
2. **Marshall Plan** (June 1947) → Economic weapon of containment
3. **Berlin Airlift** (June 1948) → First direct US-Soviet confrontation
4. **NATO** (April 1949) → Military alliance formalizes the Western bloc
5. **Soviet bomb** (August 1949) → Ends US nuclear monopoly
6. **China falls** (October 1949) → Communist world doubles in size
7. **Korean War** (June 1950) → Containment tested by hot war
8. **Hollywood Ten / Rosenbergs** → Domestic fallout of Cold War fear

### The Central Question
Each of these events reflects the same underlying tension: **How should a democracy respond to a genuine but potentially exaggerated threat without destroying the values it claims to defend?** The US built NATO and the Marshall Plan (defensive, constructive) but also enabled McCarthyism and executed the Rosenbergs (repressive, destructive). Understanding both sides of this response is essential to understanding the Cold War.
