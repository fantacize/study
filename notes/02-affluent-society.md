# An Affluent Society: America in the 1950s

## The Economic Boom (1947-1957)

### "A Decade of Miracles"
Source: *U.S. News & World Report*, December 27, 1957

The postwar decade (1947-1957) saw unprecedented change in American life:
- Population grew from 144 million to 172 million
- **Television** went from infancy to being in most American homes
- The four-engine plane was just beginning to appear on civilian airlines
- **Superhighways** (Interstate Highway System, authorized 1956) crisscrossed the nation
- Supermarkets, ranch-style houses, and split-levels transformed the landscape

### Consumer Revolution
- TV sets went from a curiosity to a staple in 10 years
- High-fidelity phonographs, FM radios, filtered cigarettes
- New products: tape recorders, boats of glass fiber, instant foods, long-playing records
- Power mowers, detergents, and labor-saving appliances
- **8 million+ civilians** traveled abroad
- Americans spent **$113 billion** on recreation activities
- Recreation became big business: 4 million power boats, 500,000 sailboats purchased

### The Kitchen Debate (1959)
- Vice President **Richard Nixon** debated Soviet Premier **Nikita Khrushchev** at an American exhibition in Moscow
- Took place in a model American kitchen
- Nixon's argument: American consumer abundance proved capitalism's superiority
- Key quote: "The United States, the world's largest capitalist country, has from the standpoint of distribution of wealth come closest to the ideal of prosperity for all in a classless society"
- Nixon acknowledged Soviet lead in rockets/space but emphasized American consumer goods
- **Significance**: The Cold War was fought not just with weapons but with washing machines; consumer prosperity became a tool of ideological warfare

## Suburbia and the American Dream

### Levittown: The Model Suburb
Source: Craig Thompson, *Saturday Evening Post*, August 7, 1954

- **William J. Levitt** built mass-produced suburban communities starting 1951
- Levittown, Pennsylvania: 16,000 dwellings on 5,500 acres of former farmland
- Prices: $8,990 to $16,500 (affordable for returning GIs using VA loans)
- By mid-1954: 9,000 houses built, 70,000 people living there
- Residents described it as "more tolerant and neighborly than any other place they ever lived"

### The Problem of Racial Exclusion
- Levittown was **whites only** - the Levitts did not sell houses to Black families
- William Levitt's justification: "This is not a matter of prejudice, but one of business"
- Claimed 90-95% of white customers would not buy if Black families moved in
- 125 Levittown houses had been resold by original purchasers - **no Negro family had yet bought one**
- Levitt compared the situation to Jewish discrimination, claiming "we did not create it, and cannot cure it"
- **Significance**: Suburban prosperity was built on racial exclusion; this was de facto segregation in the North; the GI Bill and FHA loans systematically favored white borrowers, creating a massive racial wealth gap that persists today

## The Dark Side of Affluence

### John Kenneth Galbraith, *The Affluent Society* (1958)
- After WWII, there was a "remarkable attack on the notion of expanding and improving public services"
- Private goods were prioritized over public goods
- A family could have a mauve, air-conditioned, power-steered automobile but drive on badly paved roads past "litter, blighted buildings, billboards"
- They would "picnic on exquisitely packaged food from a portable icebox by a polluted stream"
- **Core argument**: America had private wealth but public squalor - great consumer goods but crumbling infrastructure, underfunded schools, and neglected public spaces
- **Significance**: Galbraith's critique challenged the idea that consumer prosperity = a good society; argued the market economy naturally underfunded public needs

### Michael Harrington, *The Other America* (1962)
- While "The Affluent Society" celebrated itself, **40-50 million Americans** lived in poverty
- "The Other America" = unskilled workers, migrant farm workers, the aged, minorities
- The poor were becoming **increasingly invisible**: suburban development isolated middle-class Americans from seeing poverty
- "The very development of the American city has removed poverty from the living, emotional experience of millions of middle-class Americans"
- The poor were "politically invisible" too - no unions, no lobbies, no legislative program
- "America has the best-dressed poverty the world has ever known" - mass production made even poor people look relatively presentable
- **Significance**: Harrington's book influenced President Kennedy and later LBJ's "War on Poverty"; exposed the myth that postwar prosperity had eliminated poverty in America

### Herbert Block's "Split-Level Living" Cartoon (Washington Post, 1960)
- Showed upstairs: comfortable middle-class family watching TV
- Showed downstairs: crumbling foundation labeled with poverty, racism, public needs
- **Message**: Affluent America was built on an unstable foundation of inequality

## Gender Roles in the 1950s

### "How to Be a Good Wife" (Home Economics Textbook, 1954)
- Instructions included:
  - Have dinner ready on time
  - "Take 15 minutes to rest so you'll be refreshed when he arrives"
  - "Touch up your makeup, put a ribbon in your hair"
  - Clear away clutter, minimize noise
  - "Don't greet him with problems or complaints"
  - "Make him comfortable. Have him lean back in a comfortable chair"
  - "Let him talk first"
  - "Make the evening his"
  - Goal: "Make your home a place of peace and order where your husband can renew himself"
- **Significance**: Represented a dramatic shift from WWII, when women worked in factories (Rosie the Riveter); postwar culture pushed women back into domestic roles

### Advertising and Media
- 1950s advertisements consistently showed women as housewives and consumers
- Products marketed to women: wax paper, kitchen appliances, cleaning supplies
- **Saturday Evening Post** covers idealized suburban family life (Norman Rockwell, Thornton Utz)
- Cover values: father-son bonding, building projects, nuclear family ideal
- Contrast with WWII propaganda posters that encouraged women to work

### Television's Role
- Shows like **The Adventures of Ozzie and Harriet** and **Leave It to Beaver** presented an idealized version of white, suburban, middle-class family life
- These shows normalized specific gender roles: breadwinner father, homemaker mother
- Problems were minor and always resolved within 30 minutes
- Minorities, poverty, and social conflict were virtually absent
- **Significance**: TV both reflected and reinforced the conformist culture of the 1950s; created a powerful mythology of the "normal" American family that excluded millions

## Critics and Counterculture

### The Beat Generation
- Rejected mainstream consumer culture and conformity
- **Allen Ginsberg's "America" (1956)**: 
  - "America I've given you all and now I'm nothing"
  - Attacked materialism, militarism, and conformity
  - Referenced Marx, the Wobblies (labor radicals), marijuana
  - Openly discussed topics considered taboo in 1950s America
- Other key Beats: Jack Kerouac (*On the Road*), William Burroughs
- **Significance**: Precursors to the 1960s counterculture; challenged the idea that consumer prosperity = happiness; opened space for dissent and alternative lifestyles

### Malvina Reynolds - "Little Boxes" (1962)
- Satirized suburban conformity
- "Little boxes on the hillside, little boxes made of ticky tacky, little boxes... and they all look just the same"
- Mocked the uniformity of suburban houses, careers, and lifestyles
- **Significance**: Captured growing unease with mass-produced suburban culture
