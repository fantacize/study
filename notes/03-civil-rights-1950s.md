# Civil Rights in the 1950s

## Life Before the Civil Rights Movement

### The Jim Crow System
- After Reconstruction ended (1877), Southern states erected a system of racial segregation and disenfranchisement
- Segregated facilities: buses, trains, restaurants, schools, hospitals, water fountains, restrooms, swimming pools, churches
- "Whites Only" signs were ubiquitous across the South
- **Literacy tests** used to prevent Black voting - intentionally confusing and graded by white registrars who could fail anyone
  - Louisiana example: "Do what you are told to do in each statement, nothing more, nothing less. Be careful as one wrong answer denotes failure of the test. You have 10 minutes."
  - Questions designed to be impossible to answer "correctly"
- **KKK violence** used as enforcement mechanism - lynching, arson, intimidation
- Black Americans also faced discrimination in the North through housing discrimination, job discrimination, and social exclusion

### Discrimination Post-Civil War (Multiple Dimensions)
- **Social**: Segregated public spaces, social ostracism, racial etiquette enforced through violence
- **Political**: Poll taxes, literacy tests, grandfather clauses, white primaries, gerrymandering
- **Economic**: Sharecropping, debt peonage, job discrimination, exclusion from unions
- **Legal**: Jim Crow laws, biased courts, all-white juries
- **Educational**: Separate and unequal schools, denial of higher education

## Plessy v. Ferguson (1896): The Legal Foundation

### Facts
- **Homer Plessy** (1/8 Black) sat in a "Whites Only" railroad car in Louisiana and refused to move
- Deliberately planned as a test case by civil rights activists
- Arrested, tried, and convicted for violating Louisiana's segregation law

### Question
- Was Louisiana's law separating Blacks and whites on railroad cars legal under the 14th Amendment?

### Decision
- Supreme Court ruled **7-1** that "separate but equal" facilities did not violate the 14th Amendment
- Justice Henry Brown wrote: separation does not stamp Black people with "a badge of inferiority" unless they choose to see it that way
- **Justice John Marshall Harlan** (lone dissent): "Our Constitution is color-blind, and neither knows nor tolerates classes among citizens"

### Significance
- Made segregation the **legal standard** for nearly 60 years
- "Separate but equal" became the justification for Jim Crow across the South
- In practice, separate was NEVER equal - Black facilities were consistently inferior

## Types of Segregation

### De Jure Segregation ("By Law")
- Common in the **South**
- Laws explicitly **forbade** African Americans from:
  - Attending the same churches as whites
  - Using the same swimming pools
  - Eating in the same restaurants
  - Marrying white people
  - Attending white schools
- Enforced by police, courts, and mob violence

### De Facto Segregation ("In Fact/Practice")
- Common in the **North**
- No explicit segregation laws but segregation existed through:
  - **Housing discrimination**: restrictive covenants, redlining, white community groups blocking non-white residents
  - **Every ethnic group** had its own part of town
  - School segregation resulted from residential segregation
  - Job discrimination maintained economic inequality
- Often harder to fight legally because there was no law to challenge

## Why the 1950s Brought Change

### World War II and the Korean War
- Racial minorities fought in these wars and earned respect
- Military service gave Black veterans organizational skills, confidence, and a sense of earned citizenship
- America claimed to fight for democracy and freedom - racial segregation contradicted these ideals
- Segregation began to seem **un-American** to many

### The Cold War
- America was competing with the Soviet Union for global influence
- Soviet propaganda highlighted American racism to win over non-white nations in Asia and Africa
- Racism made America look bad on the world stage
- State Department officials pushed for civil rights reforms as a Cold War strategy

### Television
- For the first time, Americans could **watch the news every day**
- Nonviolent protesters looked dignified and moral
- Their opponents (fire hoses, police dogs, angry mobs) looked hateful and brutal
- TV brought the reality of Southern racism into Northern living rooms
- **Significance**: Changed public opinion nationwide; made it impossible to ignore

## The Negro Motorist Green Book
- Published annually from 1936-1966 by **Victor Hugo Green**
- Listed hotels, restaurants, gas stations, and beauty parlors that would serve Black travelers
- Necessary because traveling while Black was genuinely dangerous - especially in the South
- Being denied service wasn't just humiliating; being in the wrong town at sundown could be lethal ("sundown towns")
- **Significance**: Illustrated the daily reality of segregation; traveling required careful planning to avoid violence and humiliation

## Civil Rights Organizations

### NAACP - National Association for the Advancement of Colored People
- Founded 1909 by **W.E.B. Du Bois** and **Ida B. Wells** (among others)
- Strategy: Legal challenges to segregation through the courts
- NAACP Legal Defense Fund (led by **Thurgood Marshall**) systematically attacked segregation laws
- Won numerous Supreme Court cases, culminating in Brown v. Board

### CORE - Congress of Racial Equality
- Founded 1942 by **James Farmer**, **George Houser**, **Homer Jack**, **Bayard Rustin**
- Pioneered nonviolent direct action in the US
- Organized the first Freedom Rides (1947) and sit-ins
- Influenced by Gandhian nonviolence

### SNCC - Student Nonviolent Coordinating Committee
- Founded 1960, guided by **Ella Baker**
- Youth-led organization focused on grassroots organizing
- Organized sit-ins, Freedom Rides, voter registration drives
- More willing to take risks and push boundaries than older organizations

### SCLC - Southern Christian Leadership Conference
- Founded 1957 by **Martin Luther King Jr.** and **Ralph Abernathy**
- Church-based organization drawing on the moral authority of Black clergy
- Used nonviolent direct action and civil disobedience
- Organized major campaigns: Montgomery, Birmingham, Selma, March on Washington

## Tactics of the Movement

### Nonviolent Protests (Boycotts)
- Economic pressure through refusing to buy goods or use services
- Montgomery Bus Boycott (1955-56) was the model
- Required organization, discipline, and sacrifice

### Civil Disobedience (Sit-ins)
- Deliberately breaking unjust laws and accepting the consequences
- Lunch counter sit-ins spread from Greensboro, NC (1960) across the South
- Influenced by **Mohandas Gandhi's** philosophy of nonviolent resistance (satyagraha)
- Influenced by **Henry David Thoreau's** essay "Civil Disobedience" (1849)

### Legal Action
- Court challenges to segregation laws
- **Oliver White Hill Sr.** - civil rights attorney from Richmond, Virginia whose work helped end "separate but equal"
- Thurgood Marshall and the NAACP Legal Defense Fund

## Key Events of the Early Movement

### Desegregation of the Military (1948)
- **A. Philip Randolph**, labor leader and civil rights activist, threatened a march on Washington and mass civil disobedience if the military wasn't desegregated
- President **Harry S. Truman** issued **Executive Order 9981** (July 26, 1948)
- Officially ended segregation in the US Armed Forces
- **Significance**: First major federal civil rights action since Reconstruction; showed that direct pressure on the executive branch could produce results

### Brown v. Board of Education (1954)
**Facts:**
- **Linda Brown's** parents wanted her to attend the school close to their home in Topeka, Kansas
- Kansas law required her to attend a segregated school miles away
- NAACP and attorney **Thurgood Marshall** took the case to the Supreme Court
- Used the **Clark Doll Test** as evidence: psychologists Kenneth and Mamie Clark showed that Black children preferred white dolls, demonstrating that segregation damaged children's self-image

**Decision:**
- Chief Justice **Earl Warren** wrote a unanimous opinion
- **"Separate educational facilities are inherently unequal"**
- Overturned Plessy v. Ferguson in education

**Brown II (1955):**
- Schools must desegregate "with all deliberate speed"
- **Problem**: "All deliberate speed" was vague and allowed for very slow desegregation, especially in the South
- Many Southern states engaged in "massive resistance" - closing schools rather than integrating

### The Murder of Emmett Till (August 1955)
- **Emmett Till**, 14 years old, from Chicago, visited relatives in Money, Mississippi
- Allegedly whistled at a white woman (Carolyn Bryant) in a store
- Kidnapped, brutally beaten, shot, and thrown in the Tallahatchie River with a cotton gin fan tied to his neck
- His mother, **Mamie Till Bradley**, insisted on an **open casket funeral** in Chicago
  - "I want the world to see what they did to my baby"
  - Photos published in *Jet* magazine shocked the nation
- His killers (Roy Bryant and J.W. Milam) were acquitted by an all-white jury in 67 minutes
  - Later confessed to the murder in a paid *Look* magazine interview
- **Significance**: Galvanized the civil rights movement; showed the brutal reality of Southern racism to the entire nation; many activists cited Till's murder as the moment they committed to the struggle

### Montgomery Bus Boycott (1955-1956)
- Montgomery, Alabama buses were segregated: Blacks sat in the back, had to give up seats to white riders
- **December 1, 1955**: **Rosa Parks** refused to give up her seat
  - Parks was NOT just a tired seamstress - she was secretary of the local NAACP, trained at the Highlander Folk School, and a deliberate activist
- Her arrest sparked a **381-day boycott** of the Montgomery bus system
- Organized by the SCLC and local church leaders
- **Martin Luther King Jr.** (26 years old) emerged as leader and spokesman
- Black community organized carpools, walked miles to work, endured harassment and violence
- Bus company suffered major financial losses
- **Browder v. Gayle (1956)**: Supreme Court declared bus segregation unconstitutional
- **Significance**: First major victory of the modern civil rights movement; made King a national figure; demonstrated the power of organized nonviolent protest and economic pressure

### Little Rock Crisis (1957)
- After Brown v. Board, Little Rock, Arkansas planned to desegregate Central High School
- **The Little Rock Nine**: Nine (originally ten) African American students enrolled in 1957
- Governor **Orval Faubus** ordered the Arkansas National Guard to block the students from entering
- **Elizabeth Eckford** famously walked alone through a screaming white mob (the other eight had gathered together elsewhere)
- President **Dwight D. Eisenhower** sent the **101st Airborne Division** to escort the students to school
- Students endured daily harassment, spitting, and physical attacks inside the school
- Faubus responded by closing ALL Little Rock high schools for the 1958-59 school year rather than integrate
- **Significance**: First time since Reconstruction that a president used federal troops to protect Black rights; showed that the federal government would enforce desegregation, even against state resistance

### Charles Mingus - "Fables of Faubus" (1959)
- Jazz musician Charles Mingus wrote this song mocking Governor Faubus
- Lyrics: "Oh, Lord, don't let 'em shoot us! / Oh, Lord, no more Ku Klux Klan! / Name me someone who's ridiculous, Dannie. / Governor Faubus!"
- **Significance**: Artists used their platforms to support civil rights; music became a tool of protest
