# The 1970s: From Malaise to "Morning in America"

## Central Question
How did the anxieties, crises, and social changes of the 1970s create conditions for Ronald Reagan's "radically conservative" reaction and the repudiation of post-1945 liberalism?

---

## The 1970s Context: A Decade of Crisis

The 1970s shattered American confidence on multiple fronts simultaneously. The decade opened with Nixon's presidency and ended with Carter's failed presidency; in between came economic shocks, foreign policy humiliations, social transformation, and a loss of faith in institutions that had been steadily eroding since Vietnam and Watergate.

### Economic Crisis
- **Stagflation**: Simultaneous high inflation AND high unemployment (economists had believed this was impossible)
- **Inflation**: Consumer Price Index rose from 3% (1972) to **13.3%** (1979)
- **Unemployment**: Peaked at **9%** (1975)
- **Real wages stagnated** for the first time since WWII
- **Prime interest rate** hit **21.5%** by 1980 - crippling for borrowers
- **Productivity growth slowed**: from 3%/year (1945-1973) to under 1%/year

### Oil Shocks
- **1973 OPEC Embargo**: Arab oil producers cut off supply to punish US support for Israel in the Yom Kippur War
  - Oil prices **quadrupled** (from ~$3/barrel to ~$12/barrel)
  - **Gas lines** stretched for blocks
  - Odd/even rationing by license plate
  - "Sorry No Gas" signs became common
- **1979 Iranian Revolution / Second Oil Shock**:
  - Iran cut oil exports after Shah was overthrown
  - Oil prices doubled again (to ~$35/barrel)
  - Gas lines returned
- **Significance**: Exposed American dependence on foreign oil; destroyed the Detroit auto industry's dominance; opened US market to fuel-efficient Japanese imports (Honda, Toyota)

### Foreign Policy Humiliations
- **Fall of Saigon** (April 1975): First American military defeat
- **Iran Hostage Crisis** (November 1979 - January 1981): 52 Americans held hostage at US Embassy in Tehran for **444 days**. Failed rescue mission (Operation Eagle Claw, April 1980) - 8 Americans killed, helicopters crashed in desert. Hostages released literally minutes after Reagan was inaugurated
- **Soviet invasion of Afghanistan** (December 1979): Détente was dead
- **Nicaragua** (1979): Sandinistas overthrew US-backed Somoza dictatorship
- **Iran Revolution** (February 1979): Khomeini replaced Shah; pro-US regime gone

### Domestic Trauma
- **Watergate** (1972-1974): Nixon's resignation destroyed trust in government
- **Three Mile Island** (March 1979): Partial nuclear meltdown in Pennsylvania - ended American nuclear power expansion
- **Love Canal** (1978): Buried toxic waste in residential neighborhood near Niagara Falls - became symbol of environmental crisis

## Document A: Demographic Changes in Family Life (1970-1980)

### The Data
From US Department of Commerce — massive changes in ONE decade:

| Measure | 1970 | 1980 | Change |
|---------|------|------|--------|
| Marriages performed | 2,159,000 | 2,317,000 | Up 7.3% |
| **Divorces granted** | 708,000 | 1,170,000 | **Up 65.3%** |
| Married couples | 44,728,000 | 47,662,000 | Up 6.6% |
| **Unmarried couples** | 523,000 | 1,346,000 | **Up 157.4%** |
| Persons living alone | 10,851,000 | 17,202,000 | Up 58.5% |
| Married couples w/ children | 25,541,000 | 24,625,000 | Down 3.6% |
| Children w/ two parents | 58,926,000 | 48,295,000 | Down 18.0% |
| **Children w/ one parent** | 8,230,000 | 11,528,000 | **Up 40.1%** |
| Average household size | 3.3 | 2.8 | Down 15.2% |
| Dual-income families | 20,327,000 | 24,253,000 | Up 19.3% |

### Analysis
- **The traditional nuclear family was in retreat**: fewer married couples with kids, more divorces, more cohabitation, more single-parent households, more people living alone
- Drivers:
  - **No-fault divorce** laws (California 1969, spread nationwide in 1970s) made divorce easier
  - **Women entering workforce** in massive numbers (26M → 48M working women, 1970→1985)
  - **Birth control pill** gave women reproductive control
  - **Roe v. Wade** (1973) legalized abortion nationally
  - **Counterculture legacy**: sexual revolution challenged traditional sexual morality
- **Conservative response**: For social conservatives, these numbers represented moral collapse — the destruction of the family, the decline of American values. These anxieties fueled the rise of the **Religious Right** and Moral Majority

## Document B: The Gas Crisis (Volkswagen Ad, 1979)

### The Image
- VW ad showed a man holding a gas pump hose to his head like a gun, tagline: **"Or buy a Volkswagen"**
- Appeared during second 1970s oil shock
- Gas lines stretched for blocks; signs like "SORRY NO GAS", "SALES LIMITED to 10 GALS OF GAS PER CUSTOMER", "EVEN LICENSE ONLY TODAY" (odd/even rationing)

### Why It Mattered
- Americans had built their suburban lifestyle around **cheap gas** and big cars
- Detroit (Ford, GM, Chrysler) built gas-guzzling V-8 sedans — the wrong car for the new era
- **Japanese automakers** (Honda, Toyota) made small fuel-efficient cars perfectly suited to the crisis
- Americans began switching to imports — devastating Detroit
- By 1980: Detroit was laying off thousands, cities like Detroit and Flint were hollowing out
- **Symbolism**: The oil shock shattered the postwar assumption of endless American abundance. Americans felt dependent, humiliated, and vulnerable to foreign powers

## Document C: Jimmy Carter's "Crisis of Confidence" Speech (July 15, 1979)

### Also Known As: The "Malaise Speech"
- Carter never actually used the word "malaise" — but the speech became forever associated with it
- Originally scheduled as an energy policy speech; Carter abruptly cancelled and spent 10 days consulting with ordinary Americans and experts
- Decided to speak instead about a deeper crisis — **a spiritual crisis of American democracy itself**

### The Speech's Argument
> "The threat is nearly invisible in ordinary ways. It is a crisis of confidence. It is a crisis that strikes at the very heart and soul and spirit of our national will."

> "We can see this crisis in the growing doubt about the meaning of our own lives and in the loss of a unity of purpose for our Nation."

> "The erosion of our confidence in the future is threatening to destroy the social and the political fabric of America."

### What Carter Said Was Wrong
- **For the first time in history**, majority of Americans believed next 5 years would be worse than past 5
- **Two-thirds of Americans** did not vote
- **Productivity declining**; willingness to save dropping
- Americans had turned to federal government but found it "isolated from the mainstream of our nation's life"
- **"Too many of us now tend to worship self-indulgence and consumption"**
- **"Human identity is no longer defined by what one does but by what one owns"**
- Piling up material goods "cannot fill the emptiness of lives which have no confidence or purpose"

### Historical Wounds Carter Named
- Assassinations: **JFK, RFK, MLK**
- **Vietnam** shattered belief that American causes were always just
- **Watergate** shattered respect for presidency
- **1973 oil shock** shattered belief in American economic independence
- "These wounds are still very deep. They have never been healed"

### Carter's Call
- Americans needed to choose between **two paths**:
  1. "Fragmentation and self-interest" — "a mistaken idea of freedom"
  2. "Common purpose and the restoration of American values" — leading to "true freedom"

### Initial Reception vs. Long-Term Memory
- **Initial reaction**: Positive — approval rating jumped 11 points
- **Two days later**: Carter demanded resignations of his entire Cabinet and reorganized his administration
- **This reorganization** made Carter look panicked, weak, indecisive
- The speech became remembered as the **"Malaise Speech"** — a president lecturing Americans about their own moral failings rather than solving problems
- **Ronald Reagan** would hammer this relentlessly in 1980: Carter blames the American people; I believe in the American people

### Significance
Carter diagnosed a real problem — genuine loss of American confidence after a decade of crises — but he proposed **sacrifice, humility, and reduced expectations** as the answer. Reagan would offer the **opposite**: renewed American greatness, optimism, unlimited growth. Carter was probably right about the diagnosis; Reagan understood that Americans did not want to hear it.

## Document D: Reagan's Speech Before the International Business Council (September 9, 1980)

### The Speech's Vision
Reagan's first major policy speech as Republican nominee. Explicitly answered Carter's malaise diagnosis.

### Three "Grave Threats" (Reagan's framing)
1. **Disintegrating economy**
2. **Weakened defense**
3. **Energy policy based on sharing of scarcity**

### Reagan's Diagnosis vs. Carter's
| Issue | Carter | Reagan |
|-------|--------|--------|
| Root problem | Spiritual crisis, materialism | Government is too big |
| Solution | Sacrifice, lowered expectations | Cut taxes, deregulate, reduce government |
| Tone | Humble, self-critical | Optimistic, aspirational |
| American identity | Americans need to reform themselves | Americans are great; government is the problem |

### Key Quotes
> "We must first recognize that the problem with the U.S. economy is swollen, inefficient government, needless regulation, too much taxation, too much printing-press money."

> "We don't need any more doses of Carter's eight or ten point programs to 'fix' or fine-tune the economy. For three and one-half years these ill-thought-out initiatives have constantly sapped the healthy vitality of the most productive economic system the world has ever known."

> "We must move boldly, decisively and quickly to control the runaway growth of Federal spending, to remove the tax disincentives that are throttling the economy, and to reform the regulatory web that is smothering it."

> "The time has come for the American people to reclaim their dream... Mr. Carter's American tragedy must and can be transcended by the spirit of the American people, working together. Let's get America working again."

### Significance
Reagan offered Americans a story where:
- The 1970s problems were **not** their fault (no malaise, no moral failing)
- The solution was **simple**: smaller government, lower taxes
- America was still **great** — just held back by government
- This narrative was emotionally **much more appealing** than Carter's sermon. It also conveniently matched the interests of business, the wealthy, and religious conservatives — the coalition Reagan was building

## Document E: "Religious Right Goes for Bigger Game" (U.S. News & World Report, November 17, 1980)

### Context
Published **immediately after Reagan's election victory**. Assesses the religious right's role in the 1980 victory and future agenda.

### Conservative Religious View
- The 1980 election was a "**long overdue victory for family life and moral values**"
- GOP sweep marked "only the beginning" of a drive to overhaul American law on:
  - **Abortion** (Roe v. Wade)
  - **Pornography**
  - **Prayer in schools** (reversing Engel v. Vitale, 1962)

### Liberal / Democratic View
- Senator **George McGovern** (D-SD) — himself defeated by religious right's "Christian Voice" targeting:
  > "These people have been getting away with dirty tactics in American politics for too long a time... I regard them as a menace to the American political process."

### The Religious Right: Background
- **Jerry Falwell** founded the **Moral Majority** in 1979
- Mobilized evangelical Christians — previously politically disengaged — into Republican voters
- Key grievances driving activism:
  - **Engel v. Vitale** (1962): No prayer in public schools
  - **Roe v. Wade** (1973): Legalized abortion
  - **Green v. Connally** (1971) and IRS actions against segregated Christian academies — for many evangelicals, this (not abortion) was the original mobilizing issue
  - Equal Rights Amendment debate
  - Gay rights advances
- The **televangelist** era: Falwell, Pat Robertson, Jim Bakker, Jimmy Swaggart reached millions via broadcast TV

### Targeted Incumbents Defeated in 1980
Religious right groups (Christian Voice, Moral Majority) targeted liberal senators with scorecard campaigns — several lost, including:
- **George McGovern** (SD) — ousted by James Abdnor
- **Frank Church** (ID) — ousted by Steve Symms
- **Birch Bayh** (IN) — ousted by Dan Quayle
- **John Culver** (IA) — ousted by Charles Grassley

### Significance
Evangelical Christians — historically divided between denominations and often politically apathetic — became **the most reliable Republican voting bloc**. This alliance between religious conservatives and economic conservatives defined the Reagan coalition and the GOP for the next 40+ years. The 1980 election was not merely a Republican win — it was a **realignment**.

## Document F: Reliance Group Advertisement, "Is the American Entrepreneur an Endangered Species?" (Time Magazine, July 20, 1981)

### The Ad
- Financial conglomerate bought space in *Time* magazine
- Image: close-up of a bald eagle's face with piercing eyes
- Caption: **"IS THE AMERICAN ENTREPRENEUR AN ENDANGERED SPECIES?"**
- Subtext argued entrepreneurial spirit was threatened by regulation; free enterprise needed to be defended

### Analysis
- By 1981, American business was waging an ideological counteroffensive against:
  - New Deal regulatory state
  - Great Society social programs
  - 1970s environmental and consumer protection laws (EPA 1970, OSHA 1970, Clean Air Act, Clean Water Act)
- The "endangered species" framing was **rhetorical jujitsu**: turned the language of environmentalism against regulation itself
- Business groups like the **Business Roundtable** (founded 1972) and conservative think tanks (**Heritage Foundation** 1973, **Cato Institute** 1977, **American Enterprise Institute** expanded) were now key players shaping policy
- **Powell Memo (1971)**: Lewis Powell (before joining Supreme Court) wrote memo to Chamber of Commerce arguing business needed to organize to defend free enterprise against left-wing critics. Blueprint for the conservative institutional buildout of the 1970s

### Significance
This ad captures the mood of **business-class resurgence**. The 1970s had seen major victories for regulation (environmental, worker safety, consumer protection). By 1981, the pendulum was swinging back hard. Reagan's deregulation agenda — cutting EPA staff, weakening OSHA enforcement, killing the **Fairness Doctrine**, deregulating banking and airlines — was the policy expression of what this ad advocated culturally.

## Synthesis: How the 1970s Created Reagan

### Multiple Overlapping Anxieties
| Anxiety | 1970s Event | Conservative Solution |
|---------|-------------|---------------------|
| Economic decline | Stagflation, oil shocks | Cut taxes, deregulate (supply-side) |
| Military weakness | Vietnam, Iran hostages | Rebuild military, confront USSR |
| Moral decline | Divorce, abortion, cohabitation | Religious right, "family values" |
| Government failure | Watergate, Carter malaise | "Government is the problem" |
| Racial tension | Busing, affirmative action | Color-blind rhetoric + Southern Strategy |
| Cultural change | Counterculture legacy, women's lib | Traditional values restoration |

### The Coalition
Reagan fused three movements that had been separate:
1. **Economic conservatives**: businessmen, libertarians, supply-siders
2. **Social/religious conservatives**: Moral Majority, evangelicals
3. **Cold War hawks**: anti-détente, pro-military buildup
Plus: **Reagan Democrats** (white working-class voters, many union members, particularly in the Midwest and South) who crossed party lines

### The "Morning Again in America" Pitch
Reagan's 1984 reelection ad showed soft images of rural America, families, workers, flags — every scene optimistic:
> "It's morning again in America. Today more men and women will go to work than ever before in our country's history..."
- The ad was the **antithesis** of Carter's malaise speech
- It worked: Reagan won 49 states

### The Deeper Repudiation
The election of Reagan in 1980 was not just a political change — it was a **repudiation of the 1945-1980 liberal consensus**:
- **New Deal** assumption that government could solve economic problems → rejected
- **Great Society** assumption that government could end poverty and discrimination → rejected
- **Détente** assumption that coexistence with USSR was possible → rejected
- **Keynesian** economic orthodoxy → replaced by supply-side
- **Progressive tax structure** (top rate 70%) → dismantled

The 1970s did not cause this repudiation on its own, but the decade's pile-up of crises created the opening. Americans were desperate for a new story. Reagan offered one: **America was still great; government was the obstacle; traditional values were the foundation; and a stronger military would restore American dignity abroad.** Whether or not that story was accurate, it was the story Americans wanted to believe in 1980.
